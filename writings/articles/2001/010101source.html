2001- Managing the decline in Defence Capability

by Sir Timothy Garden

The new year sees the UK Defence budget down at some 2.4% of GDP, the lowest proportion since before World War 2. The achievement of Geoff Hoon in fighting for a slight increase in resources in real terms for the next three years only confirms that the long term decline in share of GDP is set to continue. In parallel with this decline in budgetary share goes a decline in overall capability. Manpower costs rise broadly in line with general national economic growth and military equipment costs rise much faster. From time to time, successive governments reset the baseline for commitments and capabilities through a defence review. The last one undertaken by George Robertson in 1997/98 requires a defence programme which is now no longer affordable. There will however be no new review in an election year. The programme will be massaged by the usual mix of measures which will exacerbate the frustration felt at the frontline.

Against this depressing background, there will be a number of problems for the staffs at the Ministry of Defence in the coming twelve months. Bill Kincaid has dealt separately in his article for The Source with the problems in the defence procurement area. These will increase the funding pressures in the rest of the defence area. The study into Defence Training and Education, which Vice Admiral Jonathan Band has been leading for the past year, is due to report soon. There are great hopes that rationalising training will produce a few windfall savings, as well as improving the effectiveness of military training. Doubtless, there will be some valuable real estate to be freed up, but this will probably require some money up front for new facilities in new locations. "Spend to Save" is a familiar cry around Whitehall, but is not always received with sympathy by the Treasury. There may also be some internal and external political difficulties with selling yet more changes to armed forces, who see many parallels with the Grad Old Duke of York. Nor does there seem any eagerness to finish off the work on the privatisation of the majority of the Defence Evaluation and Research Agency.

Keeping service personnel reasonably content with their lot becomes ever more difficult. General Sir Charles Guthrie, Chief of the Defence Staff, fired a few warning shots in his Christmas lecture to the Royal United Services Institute. There are shortages across the board in the Army, and empty cockpits in both the RAF and the Royal Navy. Ministers will have to tackle the difficult question of retention bonuses for key personnel. Yet the award of bonuses to some may cause much heartache among those who are excluded. The Royal Navy in particular prides itself on an all of one company approach; yet an aircraft carrier without pilots will be largely a waste of money.

The change in the US Administration will affect British defence policy as well as foreign policy. Those in the MOD, who are reluctant about the new ventures on European defence, will find an opportunity to stir up concerns in the US. They will also worry about the future of the US equipment programme. It may be that investment in National Missile Defence and B2 bombers leaves the Bush Pentagon unable and unwilling to continue with the Joint Strike Fighter (JSF) programme. The difficulties over the British aircraft carrier programme would be multiplied by a cancellation, or delaying, of the JSF programme. There will also be concerns in the MOD over the A400M strategic air transport programme. The RAF will be less worried than others: it will hope to be able to build on its leased C17 capability which starts in May 2001.

On the operational side, the demands of Northern Ireland, Bosnia, Kosovo, Sierra Leone, Iraq and the Falklands do not seem likely to abate. The desire to see an EU capability fielded as soon as possible will increase pressure for more deployments. The worsening worldwide weather from climate change will give rise to unpredictable humanitarian relief requirements. The new arrangements for these with DFID in the lead will put more pressure on MOD to act quickly. A worsening situation in and around Israel may put demands on British forces in a number of different roles.

After a general election, even with a Labour victory, the Defence Secretary and some of the junior Ministers can expect to be shuffled. Already John Spellar is the only Minister left from the Strategic Defence Review and from the Kosovo operation. The political institutional memory in MOD is pretty short. It will be unfortunate if the architects of the advances on European Defence move on before the project is properly underway. A new ministerial team will be looking for new initiatives, and the troops will find themselves in turmoil again. Nor are the other political parties promising extra resources for defence. Indeed the Conservative line of balancing commitments with resources gets the hollow laugh around the Services that it deserves. Graceful decline in military capability is the best that can be expected in 2001. We must all hope that events do not conspire to make it worse than that.
