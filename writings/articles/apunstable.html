Air Power Confronts an Unstable World

edited by Dr Richard P Hallion ( Brassey's, London 1997), 247pp £25.

 

A new collection of essays on aspects of air power is bound to contain something of interest to any airman. Dr Hallion, an American Air force historian has written many books on aerospace history, and he has brought together nine writers who are well known for their practical as well as academic interest in air power policy and doctrine. Yet , as is often the case with edited collections, the book lacks a coherent message.

 

The title begs a number of questions which needed to be addressed. While each of the essays is of interest, most seem to imagine a world where a capability for precision bombing is the self-evident key to future global stability. Barry Watts rightly questions the degree to which superior technology necessarily guarantees combat success. He looks at the lessons of the Vietnam war, but similar questions hang over the inability of superior air power to give victory to the Russians in Afghanistan, or the French in Algeria. There are wars, like the Gulf War (which is endlessly cited by the writers in this volume), where air power will be the determining factor; but are such conflicts the most likely future scenarios?

 

Tony Mason makes a thoughtful case for the utility of air power in operations other than war. He analyses the Bosnian lessons and also looks at humanitarian and constabulary roles for air power. As John Warden explains in a short concluding essay, the world is different because of the dominance of the United States in general, and its air power in particular. He argues that "In the big sweep of future history, nothing could be more important than for the United States to maintain its global stability-inducing dominance." Such dominance makes old style inter-state wars less probable, and hence reduces the incentive for nations to invest in expensive air capabilities. Warden offers some speculative thoughts on future air power roles to prevent natural disasters. These include preemptive strikes against volcanoes, and counter-asteroid missions. He concludes that "If we refuse to accept the challenges and continue to live in a long-gone world of flying scarves, our relevance will fade rapidly, and with it our hope for the future". The book could have done with more such looking forward to what the world's future needs might be, rather than assuming that new air power technology is by definition able to contribute to future stability.

 

Tim Garden
Director, Royal Institute of International Affairs.
