Mining and the environment

Mining is mainly conducted by open pit or underground methods. The nature (shape, size, depth and grade) of the orebody determines the appropriate method but environmental factors are also considered. The impact is minimised by mine planning, dust and noise control, safe solid waste disposal, water capture and treatment, progressive rehabilitation and post mining land use planning.

RTZ-CRA requires all operations and activities to be managed with proper concern and regard for the environment, the health and safety of employees and the local community.

Mining

Transport and storage of materials

Ore is moved from mine to crusher and stockpiles, then processing plant, by haul truck or conveyor. Concentrate is often transported by road or rail for smelting elsewhere. The impact from dust, noise and vibration arising is minimised on private or public property, and fuel and energy efficiency optimised. Explosives and process chemicals are securely stored and their use audited. Emergency response and clean up facilities are maintained at all times.

Ore processing and metal recovery

The ore is crushed and finely ground in mills, then treated to separate the valuable minerals from associated waste material. Noise and dust are minimised by appropriate techniques of dust capture and suppression. Final separation to produce the metal in concentrate form is achieved by flotation, gravitational or magnetic methods, or chemical leaching.

Liquid and solid effluent (tailings) from the process are treated to remove residual process chemicals and then contained in a storage pond (tailings dam) to settle out the solids. Wherever possible, water is recycled back to the plant. Any discharge to surface water is licensed, and monitored, as is groundwater quality.

If smelting is undertaken on site, in-plant health and safet)i and external emissions are carefully controlled and monitored. Special precautions are taken if the ore and products are radioactive.

Waste disposal

All waste is minimised at every stage by efficient mine planning and processing, and its safe disposal designed to maintain environmental appearance and quality. The solids are waste rock, sub economic ore, which may have future value, and tailings. Stable waste rock dumps are designed to minimise landscape impact and to facilitate revegetation. Sub economic ore is stored with facilities for capturing, treating and recycling water run off and leachate to prevent contamination. Soil and overburden (cover material), is stored for use in rehabilitation and to minimise leaching. Tailings dams are designed to provide safe long term storage and, once stabilised, are often revegetated.

Environmental management

RTZ-CRA's policy is to strive for excellence in managing its environmental responsibilities. Through effective management practices, the Group aims to minimise adverse impacts its activities may have on the physical environment and to make a positive contribution to local community life.

RTZ-CRA has a Health, Safety and Environment Policy which applies to all subsidiary companies throughout the world. This policy requires operations to:

  • use regulatory compliance standards as a foundation for environmental management;
  • seek ongoing improvement in environmental practices and procedures to meet future environmental requirements and expectations;
  • ensure efficient use of energy, water and other materials and effective pollution prevention programmes;
  • undertake environmental audits and report regularly to their boards of directors on environmental performance; and
  • plan for eventual closure which includes management of environmental impacts.

Environmental monitoring

Commitment to high standards of environmental management involves impact assessment, monitoring and auditing. Impact assessments are carried out for any major proposed capital development, closure or acquisition. Monitoring ensures continuous assessment of performance, identifies potential problems and assures communities that an operation is safe. Auditing gives a periodic review of the environmental, health and safety status of an operation.

Air and water samplers and dust deposition gauges measure discharge to atmosphere and water. The health of neighbouring plant and animal communities (including livestock) is also monitored. Performance is measured by comparing results with the conditions prior to mining and impact assessment predictions. This shows where changes could be made and enables investment decisions to be taken to maintain standards of environmental control and meet changing legislation.

 

Rehabilitation

Rehabilitation stabilises land against erosion and makes it available for re-use. The nature and timing of rehabilitation depends on the type of operation but requires a planned programme covering all stages of a mine's life. High standards of environmental control during mine life makes eventual shut down easier and more cost effective. With most large scale open pits infilling is impractical, so when mining stops they are stabilised, revegetated and made safe. However, inactive tailings and waste rock dumps may be revegetated during operations. Other surface methods such as strip mining enable the land to be restored as mining progresses.

Revegetation involves the reconstruction of an original or replacement ecosystem. Where topsoil and overburden has been stockpiled rehabilitation to high standards is possible but otherwise revegetation is carried out directly onto contoured waste rock or tailings using special planting and management techniques. Revegetation programmes are based on trials which examine suitable local plant species and substrate.

Decommissioning and closure

Rehabilitated land at Bingham Canyon copper mine, Utah, where there has been mining for more than a century.

Mines have finite lives and all RTZ-CRA companies maintain and update closure plans, which describe measures for closure, and make financial provision for estimated closure costs. Plans are site specific but have common features: equipment and infrastructure are re-used or remove; all shafts and access points are sealed and made safe; open pits are stabilised and fenced as necessary; materials and chemicals are carefullv disposed of; civil engineering works undertaken and rehabilitation of waste material completed.

Research and practical engineering have produced successfull rehabilitation programmes to rebuild landscapes and ecosystems on mine waste and tailings.

By careful planning mine-closure can be achieved with reduced landscape disfigurement making the land available for re-use.
