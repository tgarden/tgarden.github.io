Keeping the EU and NATO marching in step

Tim Garden

 

 

Growing Apart

 

Both the European Union and the North Atlantic Treaty Organisation draw their roots from the rebuilding of Western Europe, after the devastation of the continent in the Second World War, in a way designed to prevent war from happening again. The EU can trace its origins directly from the Treaty of Paris in 1951 which brought into being the European Coal and Steel Community with France, West Germany, Belgium, Italy, Luxembourg and the Netherlands as the founding members. Although coal and steel were vital to economic recovery, they were also seen as essential industries for the waging of war. Robert Schuman, the French Foreign Minister in 1950, had proposed the pooling of these industries to make war between the signatory states ìnot only unthinkable but materially impossibleî. [1] The United States had an important part to play as the provider of economic aid to Europe through the Marshall Plan. The management of this aid was much more effective through the common policy approach engendered by the ECSC.

 

If the Paris Treaty laid the foundations of security within Western Europe, the Brussels Treaty of 1948 looked to provide collective security against the external threat posed by the Soviet Union. Belgium, France, Luxembourg, the Netherlands and the United Kingdom pledged to build up a common defence system and to strengthen their economic and cultural ties. From this beginning grew the Western European Union. More importantly, the following year saw the United States taking the lead in a new collective security arrangement through the North Atlantic Treaty. The Brussels Treaty signatories were joined by the US, Canada, Denmark, Iceland, Italy, Norway and Portugal to form NATO.

 

In the subsequent half century, the Paris, Brussels and Washington Treaties increased their memberships, developed their aims and to an extent grew apart despite their shared members and common interests. By the end of the 20th Century, the six members of the ECSC had become the fifteen of the European Union. Most of the first 50 years had been focused on economic, political, social and cultural issues. The original vision of making war impossible between member states had been successful. Occasional bursts of enthusiasm for providing for external security needs had failed, given the perceived importance of keeping the United States engaged in Europe. The Western European Union was for much of its history overshadowed by NATO, but it had grown to 10 full members (all members of both the EU and NATO), with a growing collection of European states linked as observers and associates. NATO, despite the disappearance of the Soviet Union, had continued to thrive as a political-military organisation and had increased to 19 full members with links to a growing number of  partner nations.

 

The question looking forward to the next 50 years is whether NATO and the EU can co-operate in their overlapping remits in the field of security. The nature of security is changing following the end of the Cold War. Perceptions of threats and interests have differed on either side of the Atlantic, although the terrorist attacks on New York and Washington on 11 September 2001 may close that perceptual gap. Before the attacks, there were increasing signs that the United States was adopting a foreign policy that was more exclusively focused on its national interests. At the same time the Europeans were becoming more confident of their economic and political weight in the international forum, and developing a perception of their own regional interests. The relationship between the US and the other NATO members has been in the past an unequal one. Most of Europe has welcomed the American leadership in NATO for most of the time; but this may be changing. While there is no wish by Europeans (or Americans) to dispense with NATO, there are devlopments which may make it less relevant and less useful in the future. This paper looks at the factors which may make relationships between the EU and NATO more difficult, considers whether it matters in the longer term, and suggests the options that may be available for the member nations of both organisations if they wish to promote international security. At the time of writing, it is too early to make a judgement on the long term effect of the growing international coalition against terrorism.

 

 

European Security after the Cold War

 

The rapid collapse of the threat from the Soviet Union after the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 was a challenge to the rationale for NATO.  Yet a succession of crises in the 1990s breathed new life into the organisation. Although the invasion of Kuwait by Iraq in August 1990 was well outside the area covered by the North Atlantic Treaty, the UN authorised military response, led by the USA, was very much an operation which drew on NATO expertise. The formations, equipment, training and doctrine from years of NATO Cold War exercises meant that it was possible to deploy an effective multinational military force to expel the invaders from Kuwait.  Following the Gulf War, a series of crises on the edge of NATO territory emerged as the Former Republic of Yugoslavia disintegrated. In Bosnia, NATO was directly called upon to police an agreed peace. That peace, brokered at Dayton, was essentially a US formula for settlement.  In 1999, NATO undertook a major military operation to save Albanian ethnic people in Kosovo from human rights abuse by Serbia. Again the timing, style and tactics of the air operation were driven largely by US policy makers.

 

Not only was NATO finding itself being called upon for more operations than it had ever untertaken in the days of the Cold War, it was also serving new political needs. The emerging democracies in Central and Eastern Europe looked to NATO to provide them with security from any Russian threat, and also as a badge of membership of an association of western democracies. The NATO member states saw the organisation as a vehicle for reforming the newly freed states, for bringing them into the European security arrangement, and for preventing conflict. In 1999, the Czech Republic, Hungary and Poland became new members with the promise of more to follow in the future. The choice of these three countries for membership owed much to US domestic political considerations[2].

 

Thus by the end of the 20th Century, NATO had reinvented itself. Although the original Washington Treaty rationale as a collective security organisation remained, it had been used by its members to intervene, even without direct UN authorisation, to take in new democracies, and to provide the basis for coalitions to carry out operations far from the territory of Western Europe. Much discussion had taken place before the start of the Kosovo operation about whether NATO could be used for intervention without a UN mandate. The Europeans were uncomfortable with an explicit statement of such a possibility. NATO had formalised the end of the Cold War with a new strategic concept in 1991, but this was much revised in a further version agreed in 1999 to coincide with NATOís 50th birthday. 

 

 

The 1999 Strategic Concept [3] recognised the changes in the strategic environment .Its main elements were:

 

-- Collective Defence: The Strategic Concept underscores the enduring

core mission of NATO as the collective defence of its members under

Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty.

 

-- Military Capabilities: The Concept reaffirms Allies' determination

to strengthen Alliance defence capabilities by ensuring forces that

are more mobile, sustainable, survivable and able to engage

effectively on the full spectrum of NATO missions.

 

-- New Missions: The Concept calls for improvements in NATO's

capability to undertake new missions to respond to a broad spectrum of

possible threats to Alliance common interests, including: regional

conflicts, such as in Kosovo and Bosnia; the proliferation of weapons

of mass destruction and their means of delivery; and transnational

threats like terrorism.

 

-- New Members: The Concept underscores NATO's continued openness to

new members and Allies' commitment to enlargement as part of a broader

effort to enhance peace and stability throughout the Euro-Atlantic

community.

 

-- Strengthened Partnerships: The Concept reinforces Alliance efforts

to build wide-ranging partnerships with the aim of increasing

transparency and mutual confidence in security matters and enhancing

the capacity of allies and partners to act together.

 

-- European Capabilities: The Concept highlights development of a

European Security and Defence Identity within NATO as an essential

element of NATO's ongoing adaptation, enabling European allies to make

a more effective contribution to Euro-Atlantic security.

 

 

 

The final bullet of the Strategic Concept recognised the parallel post Cold War development which had been taking place within the European Union. While there had been previous attempts to strengthen the foreign and security policy capabilities of the EU, little progress was made until 1998. The Maastricht Treaty[4], which entered force in 1993, had set up the arrangements for a Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP). The CFSP was to include all questions related to the security of the EU, including the progressive framing of a common defence policy. It foresaw the possibility of a common defence if the European Council should so decide. The Western European Union was to be the executive arm of the CFSP. However, in practice, little in terms of practical military capability was generated since the WEU was so dependent on NATO for any real operational forces. This suited the UK very well until 1998, as successive governments were concerned that development of European defence would be at the expense of NATO. As the 1999 Strategic Concept made clear, improvements in European capabilities were to be welcomed, but they were seen by the US to be in the context of improved contributions to NATO.

 

Moves towards a European Defence Policy

 

In the immediate aftermath of the 1997 UK General Election, the new Labour government gave every impression that it would continue the transatlantic focus to defence policy that had been pursued so assiduously by all its post-war predecessors. The Strategic Defence Review (SDR)[5] took this as its foreign policy assumption, and had little to say about deeper European co-operation on defence matters. The promotion of the Western European Union (WEU) as a safety valve for any schemes for wider European military ambitions was retained as a UK policy aim. Yet no sooner was the SDR published than a new approach to European defence was formulated in the inner circles of  British government[6]. The increasing enthusiasm for producing a better EU defence posture can be tracked through a series of summit meetings. In October 1998 at P–rtschach, the UK Prime Minister,Tony Blair, indicated that the UK would be prepared to consider military co-operation within the EU. The December 1998 Franco-British summit at St Malo provided the real breakthrough with proposals for improved EU defence capabilities, and the requirement for Europe to have "the capacity for autonomous action".

 

The last year of the Twentieth Century was momentous in western defence terms. The air campaign in Kosovo demonstrated in graphic terms the enormous imbalance between US and European capabilities.[7] Decisions over the bombing tactics or ground force options reminded European leaders of their dependence on American policy as well as firepower. Kosovo as a lesson in power politics provided another impetus to the work on European Defence Policy. The Cologne Council of June 1999 transformed the various bi-lateral initiatives into an EU process. The leaders decided to set up the necessary institutional arrangements for the EU to deal with defence. This included an agreement to transfer WEU assets to the EU by the end of the following year. The new post of High Representative for Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP) was agreed and it was filled by the former NATO Secretary General, Javier Solana, in the autumn.

 

The culmination of all this activity was the Helsinki European Council of 11-12 December 1999. After the important philosophical and institutional groundwork had been done at the previous meetings, Helsinki was to produce concrete military capability proposals. The member states set themselves a "headline goal". They looked to develop an EU force which would be capable of carrying out corps level (about 50-60,000 troops) tasks. These tasks would include the most demanding of peacekeeping, humanitarian and crisis management, but NATO would remain the vehicle for territorial defence requirements. Rightly, the Helsinki goal spells out the supporting requirements for such an independent power projection force. It needs command and control, intelligence, logistics, combat support, and the necessary air and naval elements. It has to be able to be deployed within 60 days and self sustaining for a year. All of this is to be achieved by 2003.

 

Once the Helsinki aim was agreed, the work transferred largely from the political process to the bureaucratic. Defence ministries have had to look at how they can tailor their capabilities to fit the new requirement. The focus for this effort was the Capabilities Commitment Conference hosted by France at the end of November 2000. In order to list the capabilities needed, scenarios had to be agreed. These included the possibility of having to conduct the separation of warring factions by force, humanitarian assistance and evacuation of civilians, and conflict prevention operations.

 

On the institutional front, the embryonic planning cells for the new European capability became a sensitive issue for the United States. While it had been prepared to support moves which would make the Europeans do more in terms of providing military capability, they feared that the growth of a European defence bureaucracy would cause dissent with NATO and would reduce resources available for hardware. This was only the first of a range of tensions  and overlapping issues which have arisen over the moves towards a European military capability.

 

NATO and EU Enlargement and Membership

 

Both NATO and the EU see an enlargement of their membership within Europe as a force for good. . Many European states now belong to both organisations: Belgium, Germany, Greece, Italy, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Portugal, Spain and the UK are full participating members of both. France remains outside of the integrated military structure of NATO, and Denmark has opted out of the EU defence developments. That leaves four traditional neutrals (Austria, Finland, Ireland and Sweden) in the EU but not in NATO; and 8 countries in NATO but not in the EU (Canada, USA, Czech Republic, Hungary, Iceland, Norway, Poland and Turkey).

 

 The Central and Eastern European countries want greater security through membership of NATO, and greater economic development from membership of the EU. From the perspective of current members of both organisations there are a number of pluses and minuses with enlargement. A bigger EU means a bigger market and in theory greater wealth. However for those nations which currently benefit from EU financial help, they worry that they will become net contributors as their poorer cousins to the East come in. They also worry about the need to reform the common agriculture policy, the risk of large scale labour movement, and the difficulties of decision making in. The Nice Summit of December 2000 was supposed to accelerate the EU enlargement process. Another 12 prospective members (Cyprus, Hungary, Poland, Estonia, Czech Republic, Slovenia, Malta, Bulgaria, Romania, Latvia, Lithuania, Slovakia and Turkey ) were given new hope that their membership would be seriously advanced. The list of applicants will take many years to work through, and Turkey will take longer than most.

 

 The EU process is slowed down not just by politics but also by the reality of whether the applicant country meets a vast range of economic and other criteria. Oddly, the NATO process is assumed to be almost entirely about politics rather than objective military criteria. In 1999, the three lucky new members were taken in because the USA wanted them. Slovenia had too few friends pushing their case. Romania had a passionate advocate in France, but could not carry the rest of the members against the wish of the US. The next formal consideration of possible new members will take place at the NATO summit in Prague in November 2002. However, the political jostling has already started. There has been some uncertainty as to what the US position would be. We now know from President Bushís statement during his Polish visit that he supports a comprehensive expansion of NATO[8].

 

While Slovenia, Slovakia, Bulgaria and Romania will probably cause no problems to the rest of Europe, the position of Latvia, Lithuania and Estonia may be more contentious. The three Baltic republics are an area of great sensitivity for Russia. NATO remains in the Russian public mind from the days of Cold War confrontation as a potential hostile force, and an enlargement into the Baltic republics will be difficult to accept. Beyond the reaction of Russia, there are concerns about the progressive weakening of NATO as a military organisation, and also a worry about achieving consensus among so many nations. The one area that needs both economic and political development is the Balkans. At some stage both the EU and NATO are going to have address how to handle enlargement in this direction.

 

The different timescales and different agendas for EU and NATO enlargement can cause extra difficulties. The EU will need to call upon NATO assets for many of the tasks which it might wish to undertake. Turkey, irritated by its slow progress on EU membership, has decided to be difficult by threatening to use its NATO veto. As Cyprus becomes an early candidate for EU membership, this may cause further problems with Turkey. The introduction of further differences in membership between the two organisations may complicate defence decision making yet further.

 

 

Who does What?

 

The main area for debate between the US and Europe has been over the arrangements for planning operations undertaken by the emerging European military capability. While the Helsinki process has drawn on a number of possible scenarios, there remains a degree of uncertainty over divisions of tasks between NATO and the EU. The new strategic concept, and the experience of Kosovo, have given NATO a wider remit than just the Article 5 of the Washington Treaty undertaking for the collective defence of member nationsí territory. NATO may now find itself operating as an international agent of the UN or even on its membersí  self authorisation. Nor is there any transatlantic agreement about the nature of security threats for the future. Indeed the Article 5 declaration by NATO following the terrorist attacks on the USA in September 2001 was seen as more an important political gesture than of any great military significance.

 

Today such assessments are more about risks and needs. European governments plan on the reasonable assumption that there is little or no risk of external, land based aggression against Europe in the next 20 or so years[9] .The only assaults on the European mainland that seem possible will be from what are characterised as asymmetric threats, terrorism, or perhaps those that might involve long range missiles using weapons of mass destruction.  To deal with these narrow, though serious, threats, will require a specific but limited set of deterrent, defensive and retaliatory capabilities on the military side. Intelligence agencies and other civilian security forces will often be more appropriate. As far as missile defences for Europe are concerned, these will, in future, have to be procured and used on an international basis or not at all. The transatlantic division in views on how to counter even these narrow threats is considered later in this paper.

 

Whatever conventional military capability Europe has is likely to be used either in dealing with internecine conflict inside Europe, most likely in the Balkans, or in peace-making and peacekeeping outside Europe. The US may want to leave conflict inside Europe to the Europeans. They will certainly look to Europe for support when they become militarily engaged outside the Americas. As for the Europeans, operations they might expect to have to mount will include humanitarian relief, promoting the rule of law, and rescue of EU civilians. Any  threat to European interests sufficiently grave to warrant the despatch of an expeditionary force for possible warfighting will attract the interest of the US; and that US interest, once engaged, will tend to favour operations at the high intensity end of the spectrum using the most modern weapon systems.

 

This difference in approach between the US and Europe can lead to a debate on the different requirements between high intensity operations and operations flowing from the Petersberg tasks[10].  However, the distinction is artificial ñ whether one is thinking in terms of political decision-making or military feasibility. European nations are only going to have single sets of conventional military forces. Peacekeeping and peacemaking are both likely to result in prolonged involvement .For any serious military engagement hi-tech capabilities will be necessary but are very unlikely to be sufficient: sustainability will be as important as technology.

 

There are certain to be differences of perception between Europe and the US as to what kind of crisis will require military intervention overseas and in what form. This has been seen during the developing strategy for the new "war on terrorism". If Europe is to have influence on security decisions in Washington, it will be because Europe is perceived as able and willing to deploy a military capability of real value to the US in meeting its concerns. Individual European nations, including the UK, are not going to be able to meet this criterion for much longer.  The difficulty, always considerable, of persuading the US to take the views of its European allies into account seems bound to increase. Domestic political considerations, including demographic and cultural change, and evolving perceptions of the countryís national interest and of the kind of sacrifice that might be made in its pursuit, are all pointing in the same direction.

 

 Currently Europeans believe strongly that NATO should remain viable and that the US should remain engaged on their side of the Atlantic.  Europe, for its part, continues to be an area of special interest and a partner of choice for the US.  But whereas US national interest over the past 50 years dictated continuing and physical US involvement on the European continent, because the loss of Europe to the Soviet Union would have resulted in a decisive shift in the global balance of power, such involvement is no longer a given.  The US establishment (both executive and legislative branches) now believes that, while it would certainly prefer to have partners, it can in the last analysis pursue its global objectives on its own. In some cases, where the force need to be discriminate and surprise is essential, there will be a reluctance to involve too many allies. If it turns out that the US does not need Europe, the future of the Alliance will be undermined.

 

It is against this background that Europeís governments will have to determine the military capabilities and force structures that they require and that they think they can sustain. There are internal and external security needs. At the high intensity end of the spectrum, European forces will need to provide added value to US capabilities, if they are to be credible as partners. European nations will also have to be prepared to field effective military capability in a range of more local, yet demanding, circumstances.

 

Universal Funding Problems

 

Both the US and European nations are in systemic, and largely unacknowledged, difficulties in their funding priorities for defence policy.[11] It may be that public fear about fanatical terrorists will change the political climate on defence spending, but this is by no means a given.There are some common features to these funding difficulties, but there are also differences stemming from significant differences in overall strategy. Defence spending can be broadly divided into three main parts: personnel costs, equipment costs and running costs.  Personnel costs closely follow civilian wage costs. In time of full employment,they may need to run ahead of their civilian comparators given the many disadvantages of service life. Even in time of unemployment, there is little evidence that the services can underpay and retain their people. Increasingly the type of manpower that is needed within the services is becoming more technically able, and thus even more expensive. Wage costs usually rise at or above inflation.  The military, particularly the army, is a labour intensive industry and we must therefore expect the personnel slice of the budget to rise in real terms year on year.

 

The cost of military equipment is also large. Price changes of defence equipment are more complicated to analyse than the rise in manpower costs. The life expectancy of a particular item is often a matter of choice, and may be determined by budgetary pressures. Some equipments may cost very much more than their predecessors, but fewer of them may be required to do the task. Defence programmers have had to balance their budgets by reducing the fleet size with each new generation of equipment. This causes an uncomfortable rise in the relative support costs of fleets as smaller frontlines still require the full range of maintenance, logistic and training infrastructure to sustain them.  Planners use a similar technique for weapon stockpiles. Weapon costs show a high cost rise with each new generation. There is a temptation to keep the old stock and bring in the new weapon more slowly. In the case of both weapons and platforms, the reducing numbers of units procured further forces the unit price higher still as research and development costs have to be recovered over smaller buys.

 

The third sector for defence spending includes the costs of maintaining the support for the armed forces. Most running costs will be fairly closely aligned to normal inflation in the economy. Fuel, accommodation, food, transport, maintenance and logistics support activities have no reason to be out of line with inflationary pressures more generally. Indeed there are often opportunities to undertake some of these activities in ways that take them out of some of the extra costs incurred by military operations. Contractorised, civilianised and out-sourced support should be sustainable with level funding in real terms. In a time of declining forces, it is possible to realise some of the value of the defence estate by selling off land. Short term spending regulation is also possible by reducing the activity levels of forces, and this has historically often been used to compensate for rising costs elsewhere in the defence programme. If used too extensively such measures affect both combat capability and personnel retention. These lead to steeply rising costs in later years.

 

Therefore all defence programmes suffer an inflationary problem. Personnel costs rise at a rate slightly above inflation; equipment costs rise much faster than retail price inflation; and running costs rise broadly in line with inflation. The combined effect is that level defence spending in real terms inevitably leads to decreases in force levels year on year. In fact, level funding in real terms has been the exception since the 1980s in both US and European budgets.The 1986 to 2000 figures[12] for NATO members GDP spending on defence show significant declines: Germany 3.2% to 1.5%, France 3.9% to 2.7%, Italy 2.3% to 1.9%, Belgium 3.0% to 1.4%, Spain 2.3% to 1.3%, UK 5.3% to 2.4% and in the US 6.3% down to 3 %. Manpower changes have ranged from a drop of 25% in Italy to 60% in Belgium, with parallel drops in fielded forces.

 

Changes in structures of the armed forces to meet the new requirements cause yet more costs and problems. The move from static defensive roles, often with high proportions of conscripts, to more readily deployable professional forces requires significant new investment. Even the United States is suffering from aging fleets and manpower problems, which will need a significant rise in defence expenditure to correct[13]. Retention is a problem in the US military as it is in volunteer forces in Europe. The priority for immediate defence spending from the new Administration has been for pay and quality of life resources. Many types of equipment are overdue for replacement. The USAF strategic air transport fleet is aging and suffering from low availability.

 

We have therefore a situation where the armed forces are being progressively under-resourced at a time when they are expected to undertake a wider range of possible tasks. This difficulty is further compounded by a lack of agreement between the US and Europe on the most urgent investment priorities.

 

Defence Budget Priorities

 

Donald Rumsfeld, the US Defence Secretary, has indicated that he requires a radical change in US strategic thinking. At the time of writing, it is by no means clear that he will be successful in either convincing the military chiefs or a sufficient majority in Congress to enable full adoption of his ideas. He has indicated that he wishes to review the current strategic assumptions on which US forces are sized. He sees the early deployment of strategic missile defences, and the greater use of technology, particularly in space, can provide better security for the US. The attacks on the Pentagon and the World Trade Centre in September 2001 may have a significant effect on US defence priorities, but how much it will change is not yet clear.

 

The difference in approach to security planning between the US and Europe is at its starkest over the question of defences against long range ballistic missiles. The United States has for nearly half a century looked longingly at the possibility of affording itself protection through technology. The latest US proposals for Missile Defence (MD) draw on the Strategic Defence Initiative (SDI) technologies supported by president Reagan in the 1980s, but look to a much more limited requirement. The threat to the continental United States is assumed to come from a state which does not react to normal deterrence. Such states are normally thought to include North Korea, Iran or Iraq. Each is expected to have very few missiles capable of carrying weapons of mass destruction over intercontinental distances. The technical problems of interception are therefore expected to be much less. Until recently, there has been a degree of political consensus among Democrats and Republicans that MD should go ahead if  it is technically possible. The system testing so far has been ambiguous as to its likely technical feasibility. The use of civil airliners taken over by terrorists using low technology knives ought to indicate a change in approach; but it may merely re-inforce those who wish to cover every possible threat to the USA.

 

The strategic implications are still much the same as they were for SDI, despite the end of the Cold War and the more limited declaratory aims. Many in Europe fear that undermining the ABM treaty will unpick all the hard won achievements of arms control over the past 30 years. It can be argued that if the US manages to renegotiate the treaty with the Russians, there would be no such problem. Unfortunately, the elements of the MD system are not just minor changes to the treaty. The  ABM treaty is designed to prohibit a nation-wide defensive system. MD also requires treaty-banned space-based sensors and radars outside of the United States. The likely response for declared nuclear powers is to increase the number of nuclear systems they field. For emerging powers they may decide it is better to go less traditional routes with their WMD delivery systems. In Europe this is seen as unhelpful in promotion confidence and safety. There are also worries about what the deployment of such a system would mean for NATO. If the US had provided for itself a shield which was not available to the rest of NATO, would this affect the internal alliance relationships adversely?

 

 The Bush administration looks forward to a deployment which would provide effective protection for Europe as well. The technical viability of this extended system is even more questionable, the cost enormously greater and there would be a transition period of perhaps years before the system could be extended. Europeans do not share the US view of how to handle the threat of proliferation in difficult states. Nor do they believe that a missile defensive system protects against the many different types of threat that are now emerging. The long range missile threat is less urgent,  and nuclear deterrence remains a sufficient strategy. The costs of MD remain very high, and will undoubtedly drain defence resources from other more urgent needs. Even the US will have to make funding choices, and this may make it even less willing to commit forces to the peacekeeping tasks which the Europeans favour.

 

The European approach to defence priorities is much more piecemeal. This largely reflects the distinct differences between different national capabilities. Still, more than a decade after the end of the Cold War, the types of forces reflect the long necessity of fielding relatively static defensive forces to deter attack by the Soviet Union. Germany has a large army, and finds it politically difficult to dispense with conscription. It has focused its security spending on the costs of reunification, which have been very large. It therefore feels that it is unfairly criticised for its slowness in military reform. Both the UK and France see themselves as the serious military powers within Europe. They were better structured for the new deployable roles given their experience of global operations. Nevertheless, both are finding it difficult to provide the resources for their restructured and smaller forces. The smaller European countries are having to share their military forces to provide sustainable capabilities. The Netherlands and Belgium have established a single common naval headquarters and share the maritime tasks as if they were a single entity. A group of Nordic nations are providing a shared logistics capability for support of their forces in the Balkans. The Netherlands has provided financial support to Germany in return for a share of the German air transport tasking. Even the major players are now looking at more efficient ways of sharing their scarce assets to provide cover for contingencies. In the counter-terrorist sphere, many European nations are better prepared than the USA because of their battles against domestic terrorism.

 

Despite a ready recognition within European governments that the US carries a disproportionate load within NATO, there is no indication that any nation intends to raise its defence spending significantly. What enthusiasm there is for defence is concentrated on trying to do better with what is available now. The struggles to produce anything that might be a new capability through the Helsinki headline goal process shows how difficult this is proving. The perception in the US that Europe is wasting its all too feeble defence resources on a pointless EU defence bureaucracy is another source for EU/NATO divisions. Yet there is also a growing sense of irritation in Europe at the lack of investment, both political and financial, by the US in non-military activities to promote international security.

 

Non-Military Priorities

 

The EU spends nearly three times as much on overseas development aid as does the United States[14]. This is seen by European nations as an important element of promoting international stability and conflict prevention. In the UK, a conflict prevention budget is being formed to be shared between the work of Defence, Foreign Affairs and International Development ministries. This is an indication that the UK has recognised that non-military solutions must be considered in a strategy for conflict prevention just as much as military ones. While the military budget for the NATO nations is an order of magnitude greater than their development aid budget, it may be that within Europe the search for non-military solutions will widen the gap with the US over military funding.

 

 Certainly there appears to be a growing disparity between the approach adopted in Europe towards the role of non-military international institutions and that adopted by the United States. EU states have grown used to the advantages gained by surrendering some national sovereignty within the Union in order to derive mutual benefits. They see the possibility of similar benefits from global structures. The United Nations is recognised as an imperfect institution, but Europe is enthusiastic to make it work better. The UN has a key role to play in international security, but is often hampered by the politics of the Security Council. The US is widely perceived in Europe as less than helpful in supporting the work of the UN. This is at its most obvious in the long standing reluctance of the US to fulfil its financial obligations to the UN. Similar problems have been experienced in the funding of other international institutions such as the IMF and World Bank. On present trends[15], the European states will be funding half the costs of the UN by 2005, and will be contributing twice as much as the US in cash terms. Many hope that the US will re-engage in the wake of its campaign to build a global coalition against terrorism.

 

It is more than just an argument about financial burden sharing which is casting a cloud over the transatlantic relationship. There has been a rapidly growing perception in Europe that the US is becoming less of a team player at the global level. Although the need for the new 2001 Administration to define its strategic goals has added to this perception, the signs pre-date the arrival of President Bush into office. The US has always had some difficulty with deferring to the rule of supranational authority. Given the separation of powers within the US Constitution, it also has more  hurdles to jump than most European countries before it can ratify international treaties. In the arms control field its recent record has been a cause for great regret amongst its closest allies.

 

The failure to ratify the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty was particularly unwelcome. To this was added the unwillingness to take part in the international ban on the use of anti-personnel landmines. The apparent desire to abandon the ABM treaty and the unwillingness to strengthen the Biological Weapon Convention (BWC) through more robust verification protocols are added to the list of complaints about the US approach to arms control. These difficulties existed before 2001, but there has been a perceived move to a more unilateral approach under the new Bush administration up until the terror attacks on US territory.  Europeans have felt that their security was being reduced through a US parochial view of arms control.

 

The European concerns are increased by US approaches to international agreements in other areas. The most important of these has been the US abandonment of the Kyoto protocol on emissions of greenhouse gases. This is seen throughout Europe as a prime example of  US policy giving priority to the short term national advantage over the longer term global interest. While the environment is not a direct NATO interest (although it can be argued that environmental degradation is a cause of conflict), the Europeans assume that the US policy approach in the environmental area is all of one with its approach to security policy.

 

The Rome Treaty setting up the International Criminal Court (ICC) [16]has been another important area for policy divergence. On 17 July 1988, in Rome, 120 nations voted to establish an International Criminal Court (ICC). It was a complex process. Some 1700 possible types of court were considered. The UK (after its change of government), Canada and Germany led a group of nations advocating a strong court, independent of the UN Security Council, with universal jurisdiction. The US, France and China preferred the Security Council to be in control (thus preventing any of the permanent members from being embarrassed). In the end the Rome Statute was much weakened by trying to accommodate the US position, and the ICC will require Security Council authority for jurisdiction.  Although President Clinton signed the Treaty for the ICC in the last hours of his Presidency, it was clear that he did so expecting his successor and Congress to reject ratification. The Treaty needs 60 states to ratify it before it comes into force. It is well on course with 37 having ratified so far and 137 states having signed it. A weaker than necessary ICC will be formed because of US reservations, and even then the US may refuse to be a part of the system.

 

A side effect of this has been to galvanise the EU into greater action in the field of foreign policy. It has campaigned actively around capitals to keep the Kyoto Protocol alive. The success that it has had over Kyoto will have strengthened the feeling that the EU can act on the worldís behalf without the support of the US if necessary. The ICC will go ahead, with or without the US, and this will reinforce a view that the US is isolated from the rest of the world. When the new Bush Administration decided to withdraw from its predecessorís active involvement in North Korea, a high level EU delegation was dispatched at very short notice to take the place of US involvement. The EU has developed confidence over the past years in its ability to negotiate as an equal with the US on difficult trade matters. It appears that a parallel process is emerging in the field of foreign policy.

 

Defence industries

 

In the area of defence industries, the transatlantic security and trade issues collide. Despite the worldwide decline in defence spending in recent years, the defence industries still represent an import part of the manufacturing base for the US and for many European countries. The armed forces provide a captive domestic market; and exports through overseas arms sales are important to national economies. The needs of NATO for interoperable technologies produced efficiently are often at variance with the national priorities for defence industries. Much rationalisation has gone in the defence industrial sector, but there remains overcapacity and a national and regional tension on best procurement practice for defence equipment.

 

There is a particular transatlantic problem given the very large (and protected) US domestic market for military equipment. Economies of scale and proportionately much larger US government spending have generated an ever widening technology gap with European military equipment. This is turn has given rise to new approaches to warfare in  US military thinking, which may not easily  be integrated into NATO doctrine. The Kosovo Air Campaign gave some indication of how US technology now determines the style of campaign, and that may exclude most European armed forces from the main action. The use of long range air power with precision weapons as a precursor to any ground action has become the norm for operations involving the US since the Gulf War.

 

The co-operative arrangements which the defence companies make are driven more by their shareholders than by the desires of NATO, the EU or of national governments. This means that they have adapted themselves through mergers, acquisitions, partnerships and project sharing arrangements to maximise their business share. It is likely that this approach will be more successful in promoting reasonable transatlantic co-operation than all the government driven initiatives. It would be unfortunate if the defence industries were to develop into truly regionally based opponents. While fear of US domination will remain a factor in the approach which European governments take to military procurement, there still remains sufficient flexibility for defence companies to co-operate in ways that allows technology transfer and  domestic economic benefits. However, different degrees of national protectionism of defence industries can continue to generate difficulties.

 

 

The New EU-US Security Relationship

 

This paper argues that we are entering a new period in the relationship between the US and Europe, and that this will have significant effects on the security dimension. Europe has depended on NATO for its security for over 50 years, and remains attached to the concept of the Alliance. However, the widening gap in some aspects of  American and European interests is making the need for an EU security dimension more apparent. There is no indication that European states, whether members of NATO or not, desire to bring about the end of  this very successful Alliance. Yet it is clear that the nature of NATO is changing quite rapidly. Enlargement will make the decision-making process more complex. The new members will bring little in terms of modern military capability, but will have a great attachment to the promise of territorial defence. NATOís new missions will be difficult to agree outside of the immediate problems associated with the break up of what was Yugoslavia. NATO has made its first Article 5 declaration in 2001, but it was more as a political than military act.

 

In NATO, the US has a very special status. As the major provider of military capability, it expects to be able to mould the organisation in ways that are helpful to its national aims. European member nations are expected to sign up to the threat analysis, which is underpinning US defence and foreign policy. This was not a major problem when the major threat was the Soviet Union, and the threat was one which Europe faced as well. Today, the areas of security concern are diverse, and the appropriate diplomatic and military responses are varied. The US can no longer assume that its analysis will carry the argument with the Europeans. However, the Europeans still need the US military capability available in order to back up the political, economic and diplomatic levers of conflict prevention. The only forum in which they can hope to tap into the American military machine is NATO. It is therefore greatly in the European interest to keep the Alliance alive and the US fully involved.

 

The dilemma has been sharpened by the recent moves towards the EU establishing its own military capability. The Helsinki headline goal sets targets for what will be a very small force in NATO terms. The Petersberg tasks, against which the EU capability is sized, are distinct from NATOís main roles. The EU military structures are very modest compared to the NATO bureaucracy. The force is characterised as one that will contribute to a better NATO capability as well as giving a European option when NATO does not wish to act. Yet none of the reassurances is sufficient to stop the undercurrents of concern about the possible long term effects on NATO. Some in the US fear that a more capable EU will expect a stronger voice in NATO affairs at the expense of US influence. Some in Europe fear that the EU will fail to generate any serious military capability, while at the same time undermining NATO.

 

The EU as a force for good

 

The elements of the problem are clear. Europe and the USA want to retain a militarily capable NATO as an insurance against future threats to their security, and as an agent for promoting wider international peace. The EU, as a wealthy populous democratic region, wants to be able to promote stability and prevent conflict around its borders, and also protect its citizens and offer humanitarian relief where needed. The US wants to remain involved in European security, but sees higher threats to its national interests coming from other regions of the world. These different requirements are not mutually exclusive, and it is possible to outline a vision for the EU in which it can grow to be a more comprehensive force for good. This means that it will need to develop the military aspects of its capabilities as well as the economic and political.

 

An EU with a strong military capability could contribute to a more effective NATO. It could relieve the US of some of the burden of providing military capability for its region which is seen by the US as low risk.  A reduction in US contributions would not then be seen as a reduction in commitment to NATO, but rather a rebalancing of the contributions by member states. This future NATO might be seen in time as being formed of two elements: the US and the EU. These equal partners would be able to shape the developments and plan for the future of NATO more easily than the current planning structure is able to. Such simplicity hides a number of important problems. The EU members who are not members of NATO (the Cold War neutrals) might find such an arrangement politically difficult today. The states which are members of NATO but not of the EU (or in Denmarkís case have opted out of the defence arrangements) would feel relegated to a minor role within NATO. In the next 20 years these problems are likely to diminish. As both NATO and the EU enlarge, the membership will become more congruent. As the Cold War becomes ancient history and experience of EU CFSP extends, the sensitivity of the neutrals, which is already low, will diminish further. They are in any case at risk from international terrorism just as much as other members of the EU.  A NATO which was formed by an equal partnership of the EU and the US could be more effective, and could overcome many of the tensions that are emerging today.

 

However, before such an arrangement could come into being, the EU would have to be able to provide a serious military contribution which was of the same order as the American contribution to NATO. This is a much more demanding requirement than the Helsinki headline goal of some 50-60,000 troops. It would mean that Europe would have to offer the full range of high intensity modern capability as well as the lower end peace support and other Petersberg tasks. If it were able to field that type of capability, it would also be able to operate on its own account in a wide variety of situations. In theory, there is no reason why the EU nations could not put together such a capability. The US spends  around  $300 billion a year on its defence budget, and the 15 EU nations currently spend a little over half of that amount. The US maintains a global presence, and has three major overseas strategic arrangements (NATO, Japan and Korea). This suggests that the EU defence spend would be enough for Europeís more localised requirements. As NATO and the EU enlarge, this would bring more defence budgets into the European side of the NATO equation. However, the scale of spending is only one factor: more important is the military capability that is generated by the spending.

 

The inefficiencies of the national approach to defence within European states are widely recognised. The pooling approach to greater efficiency in defence spending on an EU wide basis could and should be done on a progressive basis. This is not a new idea. NATO fields a supranational early warning aircraft (AWACs) force because the capability is too expensive for smaller nations to field. Some nations have already come to bilateral arrangements for sharing specific resources in order to cut costs. If we look at the European forces as a whole, we see duplication of headquarters, planning, training, logistics support, procurement, research, bases and other facilities. Opportunities for more effective operation of European military forces are apparent across the range of military capabilities. Only this approach can provide the necessary scale and capability of military forces at a cost that the European governments are prepared to play. Moves towards  integration are therefore a necessary early step towards producing an EU military capability which can be useful both within NATO and on its own.

 

If such a path were followed by EU member states, it would be essential to set up some form of EU defence budgetary arrangement at an early stage. The EU is well structured to manage contributions from member states for a range of activities. A defence budget would ensure that military capability contributions were made in a structured manner and reflected the wealth of the contributing nation. The political difficulties with such moves remain great. There is a reluctance among many member nations currently to see the EU take on the form of a state. An integrated military with a common EU budget is seen as one step too far. However, the alternative is even more unappealing. As each country tries to field a range of military capabilities, it finds itself unable to afford the costs. Greater inefficiencies arise from attempts to balance costs, and the net effect is to waste large amounts of defence spending for no national security gain. This is becoming obvious throughout Europe, and should lead to a more enthusiastic approach to the advantages of defence integration. Even so, it is unlikely that significant EU capability will be fielded in under 10 years.

 

In the meantime, the EU and the US will need to manage carefully their defence co-operation. A clearly formulated and agreed long term plan by the EU would make this easier. Currently, there are a wide variety of interpretations about where European defence is going, what it is for, and how it will be used. What is lacking is the necessary political leadership within Europe. A vision of an integrated military capability as a force for good in the world could be attractive to Europeans. If it was seen as a long term aspiration in the way that monetary union was, then work could begin on developing the necessary plans and structures. The time is shorter for such an approach than is generally realised. If the EU muddles along as it has been doing, the tensions with NATO will get worse. The US is likely to remain highly focused in the immediate future on handling the new terrorist risk that it faces. The foreign policy differences are real across the Atlantic, and Europe is relatively low on the security concerns list  in the US. If Europe wants a say within NATO, it must be prepared to provide a military contribution which matches that of the US. If Europe wants to be able to act on occasions outside of NATO, it needs a serious sustainable military capability. Both of these needs require the same action: a move towards more efficient defence spending through pooling, sharing and integration of military capability.

 

There are many more uncertainties after the terrorist attacks in Washington and New York on 11 September 2001. Defence budgets may rise in response to the new threat, or fall in response to a deepening economic recession. The US may continue to build international coalitions, or move back to a more unilateralist approach. The coming year is a time of both opportunity and risk in EU/US relations. Too much concentration on the suicide terror threat could risk undermining the good work that NATO and the EU have been undertaking in building stability in the Balkans and elsewhere.  The underlying differences and tensions in approaches from either side of the Atlantic are likely to return if  further major terrorist attacks are prevented.
