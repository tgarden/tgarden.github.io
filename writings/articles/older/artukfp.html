New Directions in UK Foreign Policy

Presentation to the Australian Institute of International Affairs

Sydney 5 August 1997

 

This is an interesting moment to take stock of British foreign policy priorities. The new Labour government has been in office for just three months and there has been a whirlwind of policy agenda setting activity. I shall rapidly review what seem to me the significant public pronouncements and the few examples of policy in action, and try to draw out where, if there is, we seem to have signs of new approaches.

 

The Election & Foreign Policy

Only one aspect of the United Kingdom's foreign policy was an election issue, and that was Britain's approach to the European Union. Interestingly, it was more of an issue within one party, John Major's Conservatives, than perhaps it was between the different parties. From manifestos and from the election campaign, the voter had a choice of strongly pro-European Liberal democrats, reasonably positive towards Europe Labour, and a Conservative party which spanned a range of views from the pro to those who would wish to remove the United Kingdom from membership of the EU. For the remainder of the international scene, the interested few could glean pointers from the manifestos, but there was no foreign policy discussion for the average voter.

 

The New Government's Mission Statement

We cannot complain that the new government has been slow to set its agenda for foreign affairs. On 12 May, just 11 days after the election, the new Foreign Secretary, Robin Cook, staged a multimedia display in the grandest historic room of the FCO. The contrast of the high tech launch of a Mission Statement with the grandeur of the Locarno Suite seemed designed to symbolise a radical and new approach. The question that needs to be answered is whether the United Kingdom is really changing its foreign policy priorities, or whether it is continuing much as before with rather more modern presentational methods.

The new mission of the FCO is "to promote the national interests of the United Kingdom and to contribute to a strong world community". This is a mission statement which will cause little surprise, and could be adapted to be used by Foreign Ministries around the world. The mission statement then details four benefits for Britain that will be secured:

 

  • Security. We shall ensure the security of the UK and the Dependent Territories, and peace for our people by promoting international stability, fostering our defence alliances and promoting arms control actively.
  • Prosperity. We shall make maximum use of our overseas posts to promote trade abroad and boost jobs at home.
  • Quality of Life. We shall work with others to protect the World's environment and to counter the menace of drugs, terrorism and crime.
  • Mutual Respect. We shall work through our international forums and bilateral relationships to spread the values of human rights, civil liberties and democracy which we demand for ourselves.

 

But it goes further than comfortable phrases, it promises to secure five strategic aims within the first five years of government. These deal with Britain's role as a leader in Europe, strengthening the Commonwealth (including improving the prosperity of its members), making the UN more effective, increasing respect and goodwill for Britain including using the British Council and the BBC World Service to greater effect, and finally strengthening relationships everywhere.

Like any good business plan, having set the mission and given the longer term broad strategic aims, the government then sets itself objectives for the 12 months ahead. The immediate priorities are:

 

  • the success of the British Presidency of the EU, by opening the doors to enlargement and completing the single market;
  • an enlarged NATO and strengthened security partnerships throughout Europe;
  • a successful transition in Hong Kong, which promotes its prosperity and preserves its freedoms;
  • an agreement on specific measures to protect the world's environment at the forthcoming UN conferences;
  • a productive Commonwealth Summit which promotes trade, investment and good government for all its members;
  • a deeper dialogue with the countries of Asia through a successful Asia Europe Summit;
  • a vigorous effort to develop our relations with key and emerging partners.

 

The Ethical Foreign Policy

 

Since this agenda was set Robin Cook has staged another major event at the FCO, where he has developed his thinking on an ethical foreign policy. This took place just 3 weeks ago and was entitled Human Rights into a New Century. In this major speech, he made a strong case for the responsibility that the United kingdom has to promote human rights. He repeated the manifesto promise to reverse the decline in overseas aid, and trailed a new White Paper from the newly formed Department of International Development which would set out policies to tackle global poverty and sustainable development.

 

He then spelled out 12 policies that "put into effect our commitment to human rights". These were:

 

  • Supporting international measures against states which violate human rights - with Nigeria cited as an example;
  • Supporting international sanctions against states like Iraq;
  • Refusing to supply equipment and weapons to regimes which use them to deny human rights;
  • Measures to improve trade which does not undermine human rights - with child labour used as an example;
  • Using multilateral conferences to support measures critical of human rights abuses;
  • Supporting the work of NGOs such as Amnesty International;
  • Supporting the proposal for a permanent International Criminal Court;
  • Provide more resources to existing International Criminal Tribunals (with recent operation of SFOR against war criminals as an example of resolve);
  • Making sure military training assistance supports human rights objectives (with resources perhaps transferred to legal side);
  • Support to media under threat from totalitarian regimes;
  • Publish an annual report on UK activities to promote human rights;
  • Finally allow trade unions at GCHQ, the signals intelligence centre.

 

It must be a full time job in the FCO keeping track of the ever increasing number of objectives, and monitoring their satisfactory completion. One particularly sensitive area is that of arms sales. In the speech, he stated that "Britain will refuse to supply the equipment and weapons with which regimes deny the demands of their peoples for human rights". This is a carefully worded statement, which does not prohibit the supply of weapons to regimes with poor human rights records, if those weapons are not used for internal repression. I shall come back to how that is working out in practice.

 

 

Monetary Union

At exactly the same time as the Foreign Secretary was making his pitch on an ethical foreign policy to an audience of media and NGO representatives at the FCO, Gordon Brown, the Chancellor of the Exchequer, was making another speech at Chatham House with very significant foreign policy implications. His lecture, "Britain leading in Europe", was a plea for the public debate to start in earnest on monetary union. While he set out the necessary conditions for it to be in Britain's interests to join, it was clear that the government expects to move public opinion towards favouring EMU. He had already surprised the City by the rapidity with which he had given the Bank of England operational independence from government. It looks as though there will be a concerted effort to co-operate with the development of EMU.

 

Public Spending Review

The Chancellor has one other potential effect on foreign policy, and that is in terms of the public spending policy. Yet another review has been ordered where all departments will be looked at in a Comprehensive Spending Review. It is not clear yet how this may affect the foreign policy priorities, although there are those within the FCO who hope for a rebalancing of resources, in their favour, between defence and foreign office.

 

Strategic Defence Review

This brings us to the question of the Government's Strategic Defence Review. The Review was a manifesto commitment and its nature was explained in a speech by George Robertson, the Defence Secretary on 28 May 1997. He stated that it would be "foreign policy led". He promised to continue to base collective defence on NATO, and to maintain both strong conventional forces and the nuclear deterrent. He announced that there would be wide consultation including two major seminars with outside experts. These were held in July, and for those of us who attended it was clear from both the Foreign and Defence Secretaries that they saw Britain continuing to "punch above its weight". The Review reports at the end of the year, and will be interesting to see how it meshes with the spending review which goes on in parallel.

 

Foreign Policy in Practice

 

The early months of government have not just been about starting a series of reviews, although there have been an extraordinary number. There have also been external events to test the foreign policy objectives. The European Union had its major meeting with the Council in Amsterdam in June, and as usual all leaders declared it a triumph. Commentators were less enthusiastic about the practical achievements, but it did at least allow the new government to show that it wished to be engaged in Europe. At the same time Tony Blair reported back to Parliament his "success" in protecting the UK's interests over immigration, foreign policy and defence. This seemed like little change from the previous administration's approach which had to been to ensure that UK foreign policy was not determined by Brussels, and that NATO was not weakened at the expense of the WEU. Nevertheless, the setting out of Agenda 2000 by Jacques Santer has laid out an adventurous plan for the expansion of the European Union, and the British government appears to be keen to move ahead with this.

 

On the NATO front, the run-up to the Madrid conference in July had some difficult negotiations. The aspirant new members were lobbying hard and a division arose over whether to go for just three new members (Poland, Hungary and the Czech Republic) as the United States wanted, or to extend it to include Slovenia and Romania as a number of European states (in particular France) wanted. Very unusually, diplomatic efforts had not resolved the questions before the meetings of the Heads of States. The United Kingdom found itself in its traditional role of supporting the United States, though in this case from the logic of the position perhaps as much as any special relationship.

 

The Denver summit was an opportunity to parade the new concern for global issues and particularly the environment. Unfortunately, it was hardly a successful conference for advancing the cause.

 

The handover of Hong Kong was another event which might have caused difficulty for the new government, particularly with its strong push on human rights. In the event, it went off without difficulty apart from the torrential rain. We have yet to see how relationships with China will develop.

 

Arms sales contracts are also events which test the seriousness of the Labour government's approach to ethical foreign policy. An early start was made with the banning of landmines. Although the effectiveness of this was reduced by allowing their retention by British forces for the next few years, for use in emergency with ministerial approval. A tough line was taken on all trading with Burma. Last week, the decision on sales to Indonesia were announced. Hawk aircraft (very expensive) are apparently allowable, and to greater surprise so were armoured vehicles and water cannons. Following the announcement that the exports were to go ahead, Robin Cook on 28 July announced his "new criteria to ensure responsible arms trade". The opening sentence of this announcement is "Britain is one of the largest arms exporters in the world". Some of the press comment has suggested that it is this fact which is already modifying the idealism of the ethical foreign policy.

 

Changes in Style or Content?

 

So what are we to deduce from this early evidence? Are there any real changes in Britain's foreign policy, or is it just a change in style? Not surprisingly the most obvious change is in its approach to the European Union. There seems to be a reasonably strong feeling throughout the leadership that it is in Britain's best long term interests to be in Europe and acting as a leading nation. This will include relatively early membership of monetary union if it comes about and is successful.

 

The most interesting aspect of foreign policy that strikes the writer is the virtual absence of the United States from the documents. The FCO Web site still promotes the transatlantic relationship as a key theme, but it is a shadow of the line taken by Malcolm Rifkind, the previous Foreign Secretary. We had long grown used to Foreign Policy statements starting with a ritual reminder of the particular place that the United States has with respect to Britain. For some, it was that relationship which was damaging to Britain's attempts to become more European. Yet in security matters, NATO (and thus the United States) is seen as critical. It was perhaps strange that so little support came from the new government towards a European defence policy development at Amsterdam. Yet at the same time Tony Blair is obviously comfortable bathing in the reflected glory of a close relationship with the US President at a personal level. There are also signs that the Commonwealth's star may be beginning to rise.

 

At present we have a government that has set down some worthy markers, yet policy whether in arms sales, relationship with the US, or with China, or furthering of the Maastricht treaty does not seem to have changed greatly. It will work hard on the electorate to move them towards a more pro-European frame of mind. Its relationship with Asia is likely to continue the policy of co-operation of its predecessor, and there seems little sign that a new ethical approach will affect commerce greatly. Australia remains significant to the United Kingdom. Again, the visit of the Prime Minister to London so soon after the election was a happy piece of timing.

 

Who Pays?

The big question that remains is where the funding for more overseas aid, more British Council, more BBC World Service, less overstretch in the armed forces, an ethical arms industry, and greater involvement in the international community will come from. I shall be surprised if the strategic spending review leads to the conclusion that Defence and the Foreign Office are going to get more resources.

 

As a footnote to this first look at how the new government is shaping its foreign policy priorities, perhaps I should mention an article in the Guardian of 26 July, entitled "Blair as wizard of Oz". It suggested that Tony Blair might be an Australian at heart: not in awe of the past; a meritocrat; a doer and a practical, problem-solving politician, who is simply happy making his own history. If that is true then he should have the understanding of this audience as he tries to square the circle between idealism and the real world.
