Nuclear Weapons - A reappraisal

St Antony's College, Oxford - 27 October 1998

 

Background

With only ten minutes to cover "Deterrence in the cold war and after (with special regard to global problems and the former and remaining superpowers), I am of necessity going to have to make very brief assertions about a set of complex issues. That is unfortunate, but I trust we shall be able to use the discussion period to analyse the thinking behind the assertions. My background is as a Physics graduate from this university, a nuclear bomber pilot in the late 60's and early 70's when we were always 15 minutes from being launched at our targets, a nuclear bomber squadron commander, responsible for a dozen nuclear bombers and with my own long range allocated missions against the Soviet Union if the Cold War became hot, completing an MPhil in International Relations at Cambridge into nuclear strategy, moving to a post with responsibility for air force aspects of arms control and ending up responsible for the planning for all the RAF's nuclear capability. Some of what I say is therefore based on academic research, some on practical experience and some on policy development. It doesn't make it necessarily right, but it shows you where I am coming from.

 

The Cold War

Let me split what I have to say into three. The past from Hiroshima to the end of the Cold War, the present, and then the possible futures. The post second world war period has been characterised to the strategist by the dominating influence of nuclear weapons on strategic thinking. After Hiroshima and Nagasaki, no corner of the globe was in any doubt of the power of nuclear weapons - and they were relatively small and crude compared with the later accurately targeted fusion weapons. We can debate, but to no great value, alternative ways the world might have developed after 1945 if the Baruch plan had been adopted, or if successive schemes to reduce dependency on nuclear weapons had come to pass. The fact was that the spread of the capability to manufacture such weapons was very slow, the technology developed relatively slowly - and usually in directions which enhanced stability. As a result deterrence worked. As the confrontation hardened between the US and the SU, the nuclear stalemate prevented war.

 

There were times when technology moved towards less stable arrangements. The search for defence against missiles in the 60's and again in the 80's was happily unsuccessful. The move towards multiple warheads with precision capability might have been destabilising had the move on strategic capability on to submarines made retaliation certain. In any event, the effect of just a few successful nuclear explosions on American, European or Russian territory would have been so catastrophic (as the after effects of Chernobyl reminded us), that deterrence had a great deal of robustness. The difficulty of producing nuclear capability meant that it was almost certain that any nuclear state was mature enough for deterrence to operate.

 

None of this is to say that the world was anything but highly dangerous. I spent 4 years sleeping next to my nuclear bomber, not knowing whether the alert hooter was signalling yet another exercise, or the end of the world. But we survived that most dangerous period of the world's history. And after 45 years, we had still only 5 declared nuclear powers and perhaps 3 or 4 undeclared.

 

Today

Today, we are deeply concerned about the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction - with the focus on nuclear capability. We worry that deterrence no longer operates in this more complex strategic situation. First, we are right to continue to work at slowing nuclear proliferation. There remains the danger of the catastrophe of the use, either deliberate or accidental, of nuclear weapons. And the risk must be increased as the numbers of weapons increases. Although that risk is very small, the consequences are so large that the international community is right to worry about it and take measures.

 

We still have the 5 nuclear powers, US, Russia, UK, France and China, to which we must add India and Pakistan as newly declared nuclear states, and we assume that Israel remains an undeclared nuclear state. South Africa and the Ukraine have given up their capability. North Korea, Iraq and Iran are working hard at gaining a capability. The total numbers of warheads in the world is at last falling rather than rising, albeit not as fast as it could. The NPT and the CTBT have been successes, albeit they could be better. Negotiations on the Fissile Material Cut Off treaty will be another good development. Nuclear deterrence seems to have started working between India and Pakistan.

The only current nuclear threat that should keep us awake at night is that posed by the decaying military structure in Russia. Russia still has 756 ICBMs, 648 SLBMs, Bombers and tactical nuclear weapons. Progress on arms reduction is slower than it should be for a number of understandable reasons. However control and monitoring of these weapons should be the most urgent priority. Deterrence will not work against an out of control military that has nothing to lose.

 

What about deterrence against other WMD? Here I am less optimistic. We group Nuclear, biological and chemical together as if that made them possible to deal with in a single coherent way. Biological weapons are much easier for rogue states to produce, and much less amenable to verification regimes. Yet the threat of nuclear retaliation to prevent biological warfare agents being used is a fairly empty one. It may work in a set piece battle like the Gulf war, but is much less credible in state sponsored terrorism events, where the perpetrator is often unknown. In sum, my belief for the present is that nuclear deterrence can still operate (the India/Pakistan/China region), but for most nuclear powers it is not greatly relevant. The management of reduction and control of cold war arsenals is the most important issue, and enforcement of anti-proliferation regimes need to continue.

 

The Future

What about the future? think we shall see a continuing decline in the attraction of nuclear weapons as a national capability. Those states which have the capability will be reluctant to give it up - the insurance policy argument. We might hope that the emergence of a proper ESDI could see the UK and France making a donation of their capability to Europe - but that is probably a long way off. I doubt that the UK will wish to invest in a successor to Trident. China is perhaps the great uncertainty, and to that extent the retention of a strategic capability by the US and Russia remains sensible. Deterrence may be required again in the future to constrain the ambitions of China if it were to go in that direction. As to the rest of the world, while it is possible that some more states may gain a nuclear capability, it is by no means certain and the world community can do much to make it as difficult as possible. The greater danger comes from Biological weapons as genetic engineering opens new terrible possibilities.

 

In sum, we have survived the most dangerous period of the world's history, it would be a shame if nuclear weapons came back to bite us because we had relaxed too soon.
