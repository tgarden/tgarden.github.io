Perspectives on Air Power - Air Power in its Wider Context
Edited by Stuart Peach, (The Stationery Office, 1998), 365pp.

 

Group Captain Peach has produced an excellent collection of essays on different aspects of air power. The dozen authors are drawn almost equally from academia and the military, but the quality and tone of the writing is remarkably consistent across a very wide range of topics.

 

The first part of the book deals with the political context in which air power must operate today. Professor Michael Clarke opens with a cogent analysis of the problems for UK air power in the post Cold War world. He highlights the difficult question of the relationship between European and American capabilities. Few would argue with his belief that European air power performs collectively less well than its inventory would suggest: "a force that, even if operating as an alliance, would punch some way below its weight". He begins to explore the implications of the widening gap in technology across the Atlantic, but does not provide a solution. The other authors in this first section focus on the political aspects of civil/military relations, coalition operations and international law.

 

The second part of the book is about the technological context. The four authors are either serving or retired military officers who pack a great deal of information into their chapters. The exciting examples of weapon systems, information warfare and space are almost exclusively drawn from the United States, and serve to reinforce worries for the future of European air power. Professor Tony Mason concludes that technological superiority will continue to be needed by the Atlantic Community, but gives little assurance to suggest that the European contribution will be significant.

 

The final third of the book covers the military context with a look at joint operations, coercion and the validity of strategic bombing. Dr Philip Sabin develops a useful analysis into the factors which determine the optimum balance between aerospace and surface assets in a particular joint campaign. Synergy is not automatic, and forces need to be intelligently tailored if they are to provide more than the sum of their parts. His warnings need to be heeded by those that believe joint operations mean that all three Services must always get a piece of the action.

 

In summary, this is a serious book which presents its arguments in a very readable manner. Some of the charts serve more to confuse than to enlighten, but were doubtless more useful as visual aids at the workshops which forged this volume. It is essential reading for the military of any Service and for the academic strategic analyst. It is realistic and so left this reviewer uneasy about the continuing relative decline of European air power.
