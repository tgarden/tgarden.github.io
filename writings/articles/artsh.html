1994: The Tide of World Events on the Turn?

 

Air Marshal Sir Timothy Garden, Commandant RCDS

 

 

Each year the senior military, civil servants, diplomats, police and industrialists, who attend the Royal College of Defence Studies, examine the trends in international security. Inevitably views of future prospects are coloured by topical events. Last year was full of mixed signals. Events in the Former Republic of Yugoslavia and Rwanda gave little cause for optimism. Yet there were other indicators of the triumph of rational behaviour: in South Africa, in the Middle East and in Northern Ireland.

 

As one part of the Course each of the Members writes a short research paper on a topic of his or her choice. The collection which follows is a small selection of the over 70 papers which were produced. This selection is representative of the range of the areas covered, and reflects the issues which surfaced during the year.

 

The International Scene in 1994

 

Much of the year was spent in examining the world as it has developed in the aftermath of the Cold War. The mood of triumphalism has virtually disappeared. One consequence of the end of this period of statis has been the growth of local conflicts: old scores to be settled, ethnic and racial disputes, economic tensions, and sheer brigandry. The disintegration of the former Yugoslavia has been the most visible, but within the former Soviet Union there has been, and increasingly will be, potential flash points for conflict.

 

In his piece asking the question A Future for Democracy?, Ian McEwen examines the state of health of modern liberal democracy in this new world. In a compelling sequence of argument, he concludes that "as we watch external events unfold and successive surveys tick off the collapse of dictatorships around the world, we are in danger of placing too much uncritical trust in the propaganda stemming from our own perspective of democracy as a self-evident virtue".

 

 

Europe and America

 

In studying each area of the world in detail, the Course looked at the prospects for democracy, and the different models that were developing. The future developments in Europe were a key concern, not only to the European members, but to all the nations represented, given the world-wide influence of European economic and security structures. The question of enlargement in both a European Union and a NATO context was never far from the surface. The role of NATO in the future, and its relationship to the WEU, CSCE and a European Defence Identity, was also a key issue.

 

The continuing conflict in the Former Republic of Yugoslavia was a backdrop to discussion throughout 1994. The countries of the former Soviet Union were also suffering difficulties in the transition, and new conflicts were becoming evident. Austin Thorp in his Perspectives of Europe brings all these issues together in a novel and telling way. He describes the grand strategic view from Paris, Berlin, Moscow, Kiev, Ankara and London. He adds in regional perspectives from Northern, Central and Southern Europe, and distils the all important view from Washington. He concludes that there are enough risks and dangers abroad to warrant a greater collective investment in the future of Europe.

 

For future stability in Europe, as in so much of the rest of the world, the part which the United States of America is willing to play is a key factor. The United Nations had a mixed year with success in the purely humanitarian or monitoring operations, but more criticism in the highly visible higher intensity operations. At times, this was characterised as a difference between the UN and NATO, or between the UN and the USA. The experience in Somalia and the differences of opinion over operations in Bosnia led to increased debate over the willingness of the United States to act as the world's policeman. Larry Miller examines whether his country is moving away from involvement in world affairs in his Neo-Isolationism in Post Cold War America. He concludes that the question is not whether the US will retrench and become more isolationist, but rather will any good come from this. Among a number of interesting consequences, he also deduces that Europe will require a strong union for its future security.

The Middle East

 

Looking beyond Europe and the United States, stability in the Middle East was a focus for continuing interest. The year saw Iraq still prepared to risk a military response from the West. However, the rapid development of the peace process between Israel and the Palestinians was the key event of the year. The Course included military men from Egypt, Israel, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman and the United Arab Emirates, who all contributed to the debate. From this historic development, there was great hope that a new era of peace and economic development could flow. Yet, there was concern about the continuing problems of Iran and Iraq, and the increasing signs of trouble on the North African littoral.

 

There remained many unresolved and difficult areas for potential disputes. One of these, as in other parts of the world, revolves around water. Itamar Ya'ar has written a perceptive analysis in Water Disputes as Factors in Middle East Conflicts. He identifies and quantifies the nature of the difficulties, which will become worse as populations increase. He makes a strong case for co-operation between states, and offers practical solutions. He concludes by observing that despite all the investment in technology, including that for weapons, water exploitation techniques have changed little in the last 4000 years.

 

Asia Prospects

 

In looking further East, the Indian subcontinent was an area of considerable interest. Internal and external disputes continued to give cause for concern, and water disputes in this region were also an issue. The future impact of Japan on the international scene was examined. Expert opinions were divided over both its long term economic prospects and its ability to become a more active player on the international stage. There was however little doubt that the countries of the ASEAN region in general were set to continue their strong economic growth patterns for some time to come. Their success was also reflected in a new debate over the comparative merits of the Western liberal democratic model compared with these more centralised - yet free market - government systems.

 

Korea continued to be an area of concern. North Korea has been working hard to gain weapons of mass destruction, and 1994 saw a period of seriously increased tension,before diplomacy was successful. In the longer term the division of the peninsular both geographically and economically seemed certain to give rise to new tensions.

 

 

Inevitably, uppermost in our consideration of Asia was the question of China. The extraordinary rate of economic growth, the size of the population and the different nature of its communist government, all made it the key determinant of the future of the region. Zahid Anis, in his Progressive China: a historical perspective and a likely future , has extrapolated historical trends to analyse China's economic and strategic potential. He

predicts a continuing high rate of growth, leading to China a one of the world's largest economies by 2010. He does not see the emergence of a more democratic form of government until economic prosperity can be more widespread. Nevertheless, he believes that China can be an agent for stability and needs therefore to be kept in the fold.

 

Africa and South America

 

Space in the Seaford House Papers prevents our publishing some of the papers which looked at other regions. Sub-Saharan Africa was an area of particular interest during 1994. At times it appeared that the four horsemen of the apocalypse had decided to set up home there. The Western response was primarily in the form of humanitarian operations. The world was focused on Rwanda, but Angola continued to generate refugee problems as well. At the same time, in the South there were new portents, which could point the way to a more optimistic future. The elections in South Africa and Mozambique were triumphs for multiparty democracy. The transition in South Africa started much better than most had dared to hope. Developments in South America also provided useful examples of positive developments in international relations. Some of the Course examined the factors which have led to the move away from military dictatorships and to the regaining of control of the economies.

 

Other Topics

 

Not all the research papers completed by the 1994 Course were centred on International Relations topics. The last two in this collection are included as an illustration of the breadth of topics examined during the year. The need for probity in public life was a recurring theme of the year. Paul Acres, in his Politics and Ethics in Leadership - Machiavelli Revisited examines the tension between politics and ethics today in the light of Machiavelli's writings. He finds that The Prince still has much to offer to the modern leader.

 

The concluding paper is piece of historical research from primary sources into a fascinating study of Command in war. Ady Wray has made a comparative study into the circumstances which led to the dismissal of General Sir Horace Smith-Dorrien in 1915, and that of Lord Downing's removal from Fighter Command after the Battle of Britain. It is possible that if they had both paid attention to the writings of Machiavelli, they might have lasted longer.

 

The Importance of RCDS

 

These six papers give a flavour of the range of the topics covered by the 1994 RCDS Course. They are of course entirely the personal views of members, and do not reflect the official thinking of their governments. It was a year with contradictory portents for the future. The unconstrained optimism following the fall of the Berlin Wall had long since evaporated. Black scenarios were all too easy to paint. Yet, the peaceful transition to a multi-racial democracy in South Africa, the peace process in the Middle East and the ceasefire in Northern Ireland all gave alternative optimistic possibilities for the future. One indicator of the will for nations to cooperate and work to better future was in the composition of the Royal College of Defence Studies Course itself. Every region was represented, and the senior policy-makers of 34 nations were able to consider in depth each others' problems and aspirations. Potential difficulties ahead will make the contribution that such a course can make to promoting world stability even more important than in the past.
