Air Power as the Enabler

 

New challenges for military power

 

Air Marshal Sir Timothy Garden Ð 29 January 2003

 

The Politics of Air Power

 

When I spoke to this conference last year,  I talked about  airpower and politics. I shall not repeat what I said then, but when we look at the part that air power has to play in the modern world, we cannot separate it from the political dimension. Air power is one part of the overall military capability of a nation, and that military force is only provided to serve the security needs of the nation. I want this year to focus more on the difficulties governments are having in making the transition from the certainties of the role of military power in the Cold War to the competing priorities of today. The importance of air power as an enabler is determined by the tasks that governments want the military to do. I will therefore spend a little time on the different perceptions about the defence tasks of today and how to tackle them.  In a rational world, the increased number of requirements for military capability would be reflected in a parallel increase in public expenditure on defence. Yet,  perhaps with the partial exception of the United States (and even the US is underfunding some important aspects),   no such increase in resources has been seen around the world. This brings problems of priorities between tasks, and particular problems in the field of air power where the investment is so costly. Many of the speakers at this conference will be explaining why their particular air power speciality is an enabler which multiplies the effectiveness of other military investment. This is often true, but is a complex political argument to put across.

 

In making political decisions about investment priorities, the military argument can be lost in wider political considerations.   One area familiar to all the audience will be the impact of defence industrial considerations. Indeed some in the audience are doubtless masters of playing the national industrial card. Important as industrial success may or may not be in national economic terms,  it is not necessarily helpful in making best military judgements.  I shall therefore speak from an assumption that we are trying to do is provide the best level of security to our citizens for the money they pay in taxes on military capabilities. I shall leave others to argue why this security should be reduced in order to support a national industrial base.

 

The Military Tasks

 

When we look at the military tasks for any modern nation, they can be divided into four broad tasks: defence of its citizens on home territory; warfighting abroad; securing international peace; and providing a capability to deal with civil emergencies. You can carve these tasks up in different ways, and in some circumstances the tasks will be overlapping and inter-related. The reason that I want to use this division is that it allows us to examine how priorities have changed between the tasks over time, and how different nations place different emphasis on their relative importance. Having done that, we can see where air power has an important enabling role to play. Perhaps of even greater importance, we can identify those air power areas which offer greatest benefit across more than one task, and thus become even more attractive to focus investment plans on.

 

Defence in the Cold War

 

To illustrate this let me first take some snapshotsof UK Defence policy over the last 20 years. The 1982 White Paper is a collector's item. Signed off by John Nott in March 1982, it does not even have an entry for the Falklands in the index. It contains a one page loose insert from the Defence Secretary explaining that the Falklands Conflict was evidence that we could respond quickly to an unexpected crisis, but that our main concern remained the threat to the UK from the Soviet Union. Then we spent the vast majority of the defence budget on providing for the security of our citizens at home through NATO. That gave us enough military capability to deploy to fight a conventional military campaign against Argentina on the other side of the globe.

 

Air power was the enabler that made it possible: air transport, reconnaissance, strategic bombers, air-to-air refuelling, helicopters and carrier borne air defence and air to ground capabilities. These had all been provided for the primary threat to the UK and the West from the Soviet Union. Even with a crisis arriving unexpectedly, we could cope. Both of my other tasks, promoting international peace and dealing with civil problems, were recognised in 1982. The White Paper then reinforced the message that efforts "Beyond the NATO area" in what we would now call defence diplomacy were important and expanding. The Services and the Community rated a chapter to itself, which encompassed Northern Ireland and reports on such events as replacing ambulancemen who were on an industrial dispute. Again these tasks were possible within a defence budget of 4.9% of GDP, where 46% of the budget was spent on equipment and just 20% on pay for the over 330,000 servicemen and women.

 

Defence in the 90s

 

Move on a decade to 1992. The Cold War is over, the Options for Change defence review is being implemented, we have just fought another unexpected conventional war: this time in the Gulf. The security of the UK is still a concern, but is palpably less of a major worry to the government. The cuts in front line capability reflected the lowering expectation of threat at home. Yet the Gulf War had reminded us that we still might need the ability to deploy a warfighting capability outside the NATO area. The air power lessons of the Falklands were reinforced. To take on such tasks, which Governments wanted to do, was only possible by having the enabling air power capabilities. But for this war, there was a new perception. The remarkable success of the air campaign, both in terms of effectiveness and low casualty rates for the allies, made the political attractions of air power solution to crises that much greater. But the dilemma of the cost of modern air power and its power was beginning to bite.

 

By 1992, defence was down to 4.2% of GDP, equipment down to 37% of the budget, and the manpower down to just under 300,000. This still gave enough leeway to send a major expeditionary force to rescue Kuwait, but of course as part of a multinational alliance in which US air power was pre-eminent. The traditional map of deployments of British forces showed 31 different global locations of forces on wider security tasks, and a further 33 major exercises of which more than a third were outside of Europe. A box essay in the White Paper extolled the virtues of "smaller but better". In the airpower field this would mean the introduction of "a new highly capable Attack Helicopter towards the end of the decade". The paper also offered the same optimistic timescale for the introduction into service of EFA.

 

The 90's saw an acceleration of the process of change from the primary task of defence of the UK through NATO and nuclear deterrence to more focus on the taking military action overseas. Called by many academics "wars of choice", because they were not in the immediate national interest necessarily. We had peacekeeping and/or warfighting in the Balkans Ð in Bosnia, Kosovo and Macedonia. We went to East Timor and to Sierra Leone, we expanded our work in defence diplomacy, we taught peace support doctrine in our staff colleges. The Strategic Defence Review of 1998 packaged this new view of the world together and looked for rapidly deployable expeditionary capabilities, where again air power was the enabler. In George Robertson's words "we want to be a force for good"

 

 

Defence in the 21st Century

 

By 2002 we were down to 2.3% of GDP, that is half the figure of 15 years earlier. The military were at the 200,000 mark (a third smaller over 10 years), and equipment was back up to 44% of that budget. But new thinking was again required, 11 September and its aftershocks had changed the perception of threat. For the first time in a decade, we had an external threat to our security. Defending the citizens against the threat from al-Qaeda is not a war of choice, it is a primary duty of government. In the US this has been reflected by the significant new resources made available for homeland security, and to an extent also the enhanced programme for ballistic missile defence. In the UK, the language of defence policy has changed as can be seen in the New Chapter to the SDR published in mid 2002. The year on year decline in defence cash has also been halted for the next 3 years, although 2003/06 sees the share of GDP down to 2.2%.

 

Let me focus for a moment on the new balance between the four military tasks that I started with. Defence of citizens at home, which was the driver for defence planning in the Cold War, declined over the peacekeeping 90's, but should now be the major issue for any government. Capability to fight wars abroad was assumed as being part and parcel of Cold War capacity, and became the major driver of the SDR as a way of promoting security internationally. Now its importance is tied to the war on terrorism, and more topically perhaps to counter-proliferation and the long term threat to national interests from states of concern. Peacekeeping seems to have fallen lower in the public agenda, although the UK has made it clear that it is still signed up to the good words in the SDR. There is however plenty of signs that as the tasks multiply, it is the willingness to sustain the long term commitment needed in peacekeeping which is at risk. Yet if we are to reduce instability, which we now know produces threats at home, stabilising the Balkans, Afghanistan and perhaps next Iraq will be important. Finally in the capability to support demands from civil problems, we see home grown terrorism still a potential problem, and the need for the military to sort out floods, foot and mouth or the fire service remain. Yet of course that reduction in manpower makes it all more difficult. Add to that the disbandment of the Cold War civil defence structure and we have the need for considerable extra secuirty investment in this area.

 

I fear we are very much stuck in the thinking of the 90's. The paucity of resources, the shortage of people and the lengthening list of defence equipment problems have placed us in the position of a rabbit frozen in the headlights of an oncoming juggernaut. We are redefining the problem so that our former defence plans can appear appropriate. It fills me with me enormous regret that I am coming to the view that we need to look once more in a very fundamental way at how we provide for our military requirements. This will probably not happen until we, and perhaps our European allies suffer our own 11 September event, but it would be a brave forecaster who said that means that it will not happen. While I have used the UK as my model for looking at the past two decades, the story is paralleled in all the other European nations. The numbers vary a bit, but not much. The message is the same.

 

So let me take you through the implications that I see for western defence, if, as is possible, the threat from international terrorism of the al-Qaeda scale becomes the central political concern. And what that means as we look forward to air power as an enabler.

 

Future Wars Overseas

 

I shall start with the question of wars overseas. As usual, our thinking tends to be centred on the most recent experience. Afghanistan was a war overseas that was an appropriate response to a terror attack. It may be that in the future there will be other Afghanistans Ð failing or anarchic states that provide bases for international terrorists. There are unlikely to be many. Indeed the experience of Afghanistan will have acted as an effective example to failing states that thought they could get away with such arrangements. Nevertheless, we must provide for this type of operation. In virtually all cases, air power will be a major part of the operation. Failing states are unlikely to be difficult air environments, and precision offensive air power which can be brought on mobile targets quickly will be of greatest utility. I am sure we shall hear much during this conference of the systems which will be on offer to provide these capabilities. Intelligence assets are also vital. In the end though ground operations are needed, and the tactical air transport with supporting firepower will remain a key need.

 

If, as I suggest, future Afghanistans are few and far between, what does that mean for our own planning. There will be an inclination to leave the heavy work to the US given its significant lead in the necessary systems. European nations will continue to feel that they can manage with niche, or in some cases token capabilities.

 

But there are possibly other wars to be fought overseas. We meet as the prospect of one such war looms large. The prospect of a war with Iraq is not about the countering of al-Qaeda. It is the not infrequent situation of an intransigent leader flouting international norms, and being coerced by the threat of force into changing behaviour. Others will make different justifications for a war, but the  UN SC has agreed to the threat of military force in order to make Iraq carry out its disarmament commitments. In order to make such a threat, the international community needs to have the ability to wage and win a war. In threatening force over Kosovo, we ended up with NATO fighting an air campaign; and we may end up with an international force carrying through the threat in Iraq. Beyond Iraq, there are many possible international operations.

 

There is a growing realisation, that it is very unsatisfactory to leave all these actions to the US. We worry if it looks as though it may act on its own, but we have little leverage if we are unable to provide the beef. For all of these operations, air power will be the first show of resolve. Again the ability to bring large scale precision offensive air  power to bear in a timely way is essential. Our capabilities will need to be able to operate with the US forces from the start. This will mean significant investment as the US ramps up into its new systems. For the UK, the New Chapter to the SDR focused  rightly on this issue, but there is little indication on how it will be delivered within the current budget. For the rest of Europe the prospects are by and large even worse.

 

Future Defence at Home

 

If we look at the other driver in future security needs, the defence of home territory, it appears that only the US is taking the investment needs seriously. Whether defences against ballistic missiles are the most urgent requirements is arguable, but you have to be impressed by the scale of resources going down that project. We will also see defences against cruise missiles, which are a much more urgent threat, becoming an issue.

 

The other side of the coin is what role the military in general, and air power in particular, will have in the wider field of what the US calls homeland security. The air power aspects are likely to be relatively limited. Currently, QRA air defence gives some public reassurance, although little practical security. Perhaps more important is the role of aerial intelligence systems. These have utility for counter-terrorism just as for war fighting and peacekeeping. We see in the UK, what is currently a rather half hearted use of the Reserves for home defence duties. This may need to become more serious, and will then start to make another call on defence resources. Military helicopter transport will be key to successful consequence management of many major terrorist events. Given that we would need a pool ready to react, this may mean an expansion is needed in this area of military activity.

 

There is much that needs to be done in terms of investment in the non-military aspects of local emergency response. We were prepared to invest a lot in civil defence in the Cold War, and we may come full circle in this.

 

Future Peace Support Operations

 

The stabilising operations around the world will still be needed, particularly for post conflict situations. While these are not at the cutting edge of air power technology, they must be considered in terms of resources and balance of capabilities. We have not finished yet in the Balkans or Afghanistan. We are now talking about the post-conflict requirements in Iraq. None of these important tasks  are cost free, and emphasis on providing the necessary manpower for what are labour intensive long term operations will reduce the money available for transformation technologies and equipment at the warfighting end of the spectrum.

 

Squaring the Circle

 

The list of key air power capabilities for these increasing tasks is well known. Providing them within a useful timescale remains the problem. When we look at the overall plan, we should assess what must be provided nationally, what could be shared with others, and where we can rely on allies or supranational bodies to provide an enabling capability for all.

 

I was involved in research work ahead of the Prague summit to look at ways that NATO might work with the grain of Europe and the new security needs to keep up with the new transformation initiatives in the US, which General Jumper will talk about next. There is a real problem, and it is not just about unwillingness to spend money on defence in Europe, or even about the penny packeting of research and of capabilities Ð although both of those are important. There is a growing divergence  of strategic approach between the mainland of Europe and the US with the UK. The UK has made it clear that it wants to get aboard the transformation technology train, but it will find it difficult to afford on its own. While co-operative solutions would be possible with European partners, they are less certain that this is the most important way to be going.   If the threat is from al Qaeda and asymmetric warfare, and the US will in any case provide the modern high intensity warfare capabilities, they are reluctant to become bit players in the new US strategic concept.

 

Prague was officially a great success, but as those who were involved know, there is a lot of diplomatic papering over the cracks. The proposal for the NATO Response Force was seen as one way to help European members adopt the new US transformation technologies.  The measure is, in my view, unlikely to do much in that respect, and will be very slow to act. If NATO is to remain relevant, it will have to field capabilities as NATO that are important to the US. Currently only NATO AWACs fits that bill. If Europeans are to field modern airpower enabling capabilities, they are going to have to do it collectively. Only NATO AWACS fits that description currently. The possibility of a NATO JSTARS is a real hope that it may be possible to do something, but we need to see that sort of approach extend into the rest of the airpower enablers, and much more urgently than a decade away. Only by pooling of European capabilities will we be able to meet the extraordinary range of security challenges that we face, and remain able to operate with the most important air power provider, the USA.
