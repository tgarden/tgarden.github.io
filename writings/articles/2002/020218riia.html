EUROPE AND AMERICA: A NEW STRATEGIC PARTNERSHIP

What Can we Afford?

A panel contribution by Tim Garden

 

The question of what we can afford in military capability should shape all our thinking. It is too easy to  make grandiose statements about our responsibilities for promoting peace and security across the world's stage. Of course, the vision ñ or what we used to call foreign policy ñ is important, and should help to define the kind of capabilities to which we aspire. But the constraints are always in the end those of resources. Our conference is discussing Europe and America, and we look for their common interests and worry about differences in approach. It is usually claimed that the big divide across the Atlantic is caused by the different scale of the defence budgets. This is only partially true. While the US and the EU have broadly similar GDPs, the US is now spending nearly twice the number of dollars (helped by the Euro/Dollar rate) on military aspects of security as do the 15 nations of the EU combined. On the other hand, the EU spends three times the US budget on the development aid which also contributes to international peace and security.

 

The real problem in Europe is not the amount that we allocate to military spending, but the way in which we spend it. This is also to an extent true of the USA, and I will return to that in a moment. Europe (and throughout this I shall mean the EU when I say Europe), spends around Ä150 bn a year on defence which is enough to have nearly 2 million soldiers in uniform and over 3000 combat aircraft. Yet when it comes to sorting out a relatively small problem in its backyard ñ The Balkans ñ it finds itself able to do little but act in a supporting role to the US, when military action is called for. European governments have recognised this by agreeing the Defence Capabilities Initiative in NATO in April 1999, and subsequently the Helsinki Headline Goals for an EU flexible military capability. After 3 years the DCI has achieved little, and the HHG process has done little more than temporarily arrest the continuing decline in national defence budgets. Funding the key enabling capabilities still remains unsolved. There is no mystery about the restructuring of military capabilities that is needed throughout Europe, but national, institutional, political and industrial vested interests make progress towards more useful and cost effective military capability painfully slow.

 

All militaries suffer from the public sector problem. Industrial wages rise faster than domestic inflation, reflecting increases in productivity and national wealth. Military pay must keep up if people are to be recruited and retained, but for many military activities numbers of people are not amenable to reductions through increase in productivity. Indeed for the new tasks, which armies find themselves doing, there may be a need for increasing numbers of troops on the ground. So the pay bill rises faster than inflation. The equipment side also suffers from a different rate of inflation. The calculations are complex, as new weapon systems can show productivity increases (precision weapons reduce sortie requirements), and defence programmers can adjust their replacement timings to match affordability. All that said, the audience here will know that the rising unit cost of the small runs of modern weapon systems is a key concern. National industrial interests compound the problem by making production runs even smaller, and also generating interoperability problems. All of this means that around 80% of the defence budget is subject to cost rises year on year which are greater than domestic inflation. European governments think they have done well if they hold their defence budgets level in real terms, and so year on year the capability declines; and the share of GDP given to defence reflects this comparative decline. In the Cold War, at times when the UK was in economic trouble, it felt able to spend over 5% of GDP on Defence. Today, when we are rich, wanting to do good around the world and arguably newly vulnerable, we are prepared to pay less than half that proportion of our national income. The story is the same throughout Europe.

 

But as I said, it is not just how little we spend, it is also how badly we spend it. 15 nations duplicating all their defence activities. If we were a multinational business, which in a sense we are, we would long ago have rationalised our supply chains, our training, our product delivery systems, and done away with the bureaucracy of the headquarters in every country. This is politically very difficult for member states. Michael Alexander and I wrote an article (The arithmetic of defence policy) in last July's International Affairs, which concluded:

 

If the nation states of Europe wish to retain something approaching their current ability to use military force in defence of their interests over the next 15 to 20 years, they will have to be prepared to integrate their respective efforts to an ever more substantial degree.  The pursuit on a national basis of sophisticated and balanced capabilities, suitable for both high intensity and peacekeeping operations, is a chimera. In the case of the UK we will hit the wall well before the first new aircraft carrier is due to arrive in 2012. In the case of most of our European partners, the crunch will come much sooner.

 

This analysis was before the added security needs of 11 September were apparent. While I hold out little hope for fast progress in the rationalisation of EU defence, I still believe that some things can be done to start the process, and to begin to convince finance ministries that this is the only way.

 

Let me now turn to the USA. It is often assumed that the resources for defence are so enormous, the research investment so great, and the technology so far ahead of all other nations, that the US has no problems. Of course this is far from true. While the US does not suffer from the fractured duplication of EU states, it has all of the other problems. Its defence spending has also more than  halved as a proportion of GDP. It averaged 8% over the past 60 years and will be 2.9% this year. The GAO quantified the shortfall at $50bn a year just to sustain the current capabilities. The equipment costs using cutting edge technologies rise rapidly, and the luxury of many different aircraft doing similar jobs is disappearing. Industrial and other interests are subject to pork barrel politics, and may mean less than optimum procurement or basing.

 

The QDR report, published at the end of September, identifies problems with readiness, retention of people, poor training facilities, sustained underfunding of infrastructure maintenance, and a lack of investment in new equipment, which is described as living off the fat of the 80's. These problems will be well known in the UK and other European defence establishments. The QDR does not detail the force structures that will be developed in any but the most general terms. Countering CBRNE threats is a recurring theme. For those unfamiliar with this acronym it stands for chemical, biological, radiological, nuclear, and enhanced high explosive weapons. Developments in space capabilities including control of space aspects are trailed. Intelligence is seen as a key enabler.  To the European reader, the vision of global reach supported by extraordinary technologies and massive firepower makes the practicality  of future alliance operations seem unlikely. While the QDR constantly refers to helping allies and friends, it gives no clue as to how the widening gap in capabilities between the US and the rest of the world will be bridged. But these plans may take far longer to come into being than anyone predicts.

 

On 10 January. President Bush signed the Defense funding bill for 2002. It was for a rise of $30 bn over the previous year. Just over half of that increase goes on missile defence and personnel retention, which leaves nothing like enough to cover the shortfalls that were identified a year before. As public finance becomes ever more difficult in the USA, we may find that the timescales for these grandiose plans begins to lengthen. As the New York Times pointed out in the middle of this month, Mr Rumsfeld has largely bowed to the momentum of familiar weapons programmes.

 

What does affordability mean to the question of a European and American strategic partnership. The US will have problems with moving as fast as it would wish. But it starts from a much better position, and is investing far more in military research and development than Europe. Most EU forces are irrelevant to US needs on the types of operations that America wishes to undertake. The Europeans will fall further and faster behind unless they both increase spending and rationalise between themselves. As neither of these are likely to happen, the tasking will fall quite naturally into a division of labour between the US and Europe. The US will look after long range high intensity power projection, and Europe will look after the post conflict rebuilding military activities. This will not be because a strategic choice has been made: affordability will determine this. The implications for NATO and transatlantic relations may be profound.
