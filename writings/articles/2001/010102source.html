2001 - New problems for the FCO?

by Sir Timothy Garden

 

As FCO officials return to their offices after the long Christmas break, there will be no lack of issues for the New Year. Two elections will dominate their assessments. The first has finally stumbled to a conclusion: George W Bush will be setting the transatlantic agenda. The date of the second has still to be announced: the timing of the UK General Election will affect both foreign policy and its implementation. Europe is bound to be a major issue in the British election, and this will make Ministers less willing to take controversial steps. There are also enough unresolved and emerging problems around the world to ensure that there will be some unpleasant surprises during the year.

Brainwashed by CNN, many US citizens believe that the world's trouble spots are exclusively patrolled by Americans. We sometimes suffer from similar delusions in Britain about our own forces. The new Bush administration is likely to seek a reduction to this burden. They may start with a timetable for full withdrawal of US forces in the Balkans This will be the first of a number of issues that may rattle the transatlantic relationship.

Warning US shots across the bows of the European Union defence project have been sounding since the Nice summit. America will not wish to risk its leadership role in European security. NATO remains their key institution for ensuring that Europe does not wander too far from the US security agenda. The European capability which is developing out of the headline goals agreed by EU states at Helsinki in December 1999 will be a policy issue for the new Bush administration. This may give Tony Blair some headaches in a run up to his election.

One other NATO issue will be a source for transatlantic tension: the next stage of enlargement.. With EU enlargement receiving a slightly cloudy green light at the Nice summit, the parallel NATO process is likely to become an issue for this year. Romania and Slovenia are left over business from 1999. There is a strategic argument for filling in some of the gaps on the NATO map, which would include Slovakia and Bulgaria. However, the Baltic States will be an issue to be resolved. The Bush administration will not wish to defer to Russian sensitivities, and may want to include some or all of Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania in the next tranche of NATO enlargement. This may not be the way some European states see the future security of the region being best served.

A more confrontational approach to Russia may also develop over the proposed National Missile Defence system. The Bush administration is deeply committed to moving forward with the NMD project. They are prepared to exercise their right to withdraw from the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty if the Russians will not agree a suitable amendment to it. Some European governments have already indicated their opposition to NMD. The UK has a direct part to play in any future missile defence architecture. The early warning radar at Fylingdales will need to be rebuilt to house a large radar array in direct contravention of the ABM treaty as currently written. Should it allow the necessary installations as part of the special relationship with the US; while accepting that this might put the UK at risk of preemptive attack? Or should it adopt a position in common with its EU colleagues which recognises the adverse impact of US NMD on European security; while accepting that it will lose significant clout with the US as a result?

One region which is certain to cause difficulties is Israel and its neighbours. Again, the change of US Presidency is important. Clinton has worked hard to find common ground between Israel and the Palestinian Authority. Bush will have other early priorities, and there is an Israeli election to confuse the issue further. Nor does Europe take the same view as the US about the rights of each side. It may be that the region will become the focus for much greater violence in the coming year if no settlement can be agreed. It would not be a rerun of the Gulf War of ten years ago - the Arab neighbours would side with the Palestinians, while the US (probably without the support of Europe) came to the defence of Israel. This would make for a very dangerous international situation.

There are plenty of other potential trouble spots for 2001: Taiwan, Indonesia, Columbia, Korea, the Indian subcontinent and many regions of Africa. Perhaps, the one bright spot is the reduced risk of further conflict in the Balkans. Provided that the hard slog of post-conflict rebuilding of the economy and society continues, there is real hope for a brighter future in the much longer term. Russia will continue to need careful attention, and increasingly China will have to be reckoned with.

Even in the peaceful area of Europe, foreign policy will be beset by challenges. Monetary Union will be the most difficult policy area in an election year, yet the Labour Government will wish to make progress if only to retain influence in the EU. Although at this stage it seems improbable that the British election will bring a Conservative government into being, that really could bring about some policy crises over Europe.

All in all, 2001 will be a busy year for the Foreign Secretary and FCO officials. Trying to keep the special relationship with the US alive will be particularly difficult, and there may be real choices to be made between loyalty to Washington or to Brussels.
