The Issues for 1997

Remarks to London Correspondents 19 December 1996

It struck me as I started thinking about today's remit, that seemed to have spent a remarkable amount of time this year travelling to conferences, seminars and workshops which had been called to worry about the future of European security. And after a year of such debate, I find it difficult to get too worried. Margot has asked us each to forecast the key defence issues for 1997, and it could be fun to speculate about all the terrifying things that just might happen. I am not going to do that because it seems to me that we are getting better at managing emerging crises, and from a European perspective, we have never been safer. That is not to say that there are not some difficult security issues which will need sorting out fairly soon. I shall leave the post IFOR arrangements to others, but in any event I do not see Bosnia as a major problem in 97 - perhaps the following year. I worry some about the possibilities of conflict in and around Israel, but there is still room for good sense to prevail. Cyprus is not without danger, but should be manageable. Nor will I use the wider definition of security, for the 10 minutes that I have. There are certainly more dangers from criminality, corruption and environmental problems than from classic military threats. For the UK, and some other parts of Europe, terrorism will remain uppermost as a security concern. We can if you wish pick any of these up in the discussion.

I shall concentrate on where NATO is going and how it can manage the difficulties of the coming year. I think NATO has three challenges - Enlargement, restructuring and resources - and they are all to a degree interlinked.

 

I think you can argue that the Bosnia problem has kept NATO members interested and engaged. As the involvement declines, there will be a return to a number of key issues which are coming to a head. At the front of these is the question of enlargement, I see this morning's post brought another seminar on the subject. Although, the security and foreign policy specialists are all only too aware of this looming problem, there is little public debate or interest at present. The common wisdom is that 3 or 4 states will be invited to become full members in a first tranche. They are likely to be Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic and perhaps Slovenia. That would leave relationships with 3 rather different categories of European states to be addressed. There are those who do not meet the membership guidelines yet for various economic and political reasons such as Bulgaria and Romania and Slovakia; there are those that could probably meet the guidelines in the near term but would cause particular tensions - and here I think of the Baltic States, and then there is Russia, Belo-Russia and the Ukraine.

 

The impetus, from the United States, to get on with early enlargement is probably unstoppable. The difficulties in the process will come from the attitude Russia takes, the requirement for extra resources and the need for national approval. At a NATO seminar last week, a Russian parliamentarian suggested that NATO should offer membership on equal terms to Russia as it started enlargement. He claimed that it would be a long time before Russia qualified, and it might not even want to join, but it should be accorded the special status that it deserved. I was in the Ukraine 3 weeks ago, and there the signals were much more confused, The debate was over whether Ukraine should be looking East or West or ploughing an independent security role. There was some feeling that they should be recognised for having given up their nuclear capability. The suggestion raised there was for a central European NFZ, and it seemed to have some attractions. The NATO speakers showed little sign of flexibility.

 

So the key debate, will be on how to manage enlargement, what to do with the PFP states that are left out in the first tranche, and how to manage relationships with Russia. In the background the IGC of the EU will be continuing to look for progress towards a european foreign and security policy, and the means for carrying out. I do not expect that this will bring exciting developments in the next 12 months, and I shall not therefore discuss it further.

 

At the same time that NATO is looking at enlargement, it also needs to get on with restructuring. All agree that it needs to be more streamlined and make savings in its overheads. Yet the most likely outcome will be the elimination of the recently created HQ AFNORWEST in favour of AFCENT and AFSOUTH, thus rewarding the least efficient at the expense of the most. Accession of new members, and the return of France, will also increase pressures for representation at every level, and cause more growth of HQ.

 

And that brings me to my third security issue for 1997, the question of resources. Nations, having taken significant force cuts at the end of the Cold War, appear to be continuing to redefine their defence needs downwards. If I take the UK case, which I know best, we saw cuts of around 30% in combat capability in the early 90s. We then had another exercise to cut support costs by using private companies to do support tasks which further reduced the numbers of people in the armed forces. If the election is won by the Labour Party, they have promised a full scale review of Defence within 6 months of taking office. Small wonder that retention of trained manpower is proving difficult. Similar stories across the NATO membership, and France is an interesting example, suggest that NATO when it enlarges will take on a greater security obligation at the same time as its members are reducing the resources they are prepared to devote to defence.

 

As a final security issue for 1997, perhaps I can add that much depends on the role that the United States wishes to play. The preservation of NATO is important for European security because it provides the linkage with the US as well as providing a real military capability. I have no reason to suppose that next year will be any different from this, but I suppose conflict in Israel could strain the US/European relationship more than usual.
