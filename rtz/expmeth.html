Exploration methods

All the information available about a region is studied. Exploration "targets" are selected by comparison with regions where deposits have been found or where the geology is believed favourable. The possible location of orebodies is constantly modified by research and practical experience. Improved understanding of prospective environments may prompt renewed interest in previously discarded areas. Satellite and other remote sensing data enable regions to be studied and analysed for patterns and structures which give clues to mineralisation. For example, infra-red imagery assists classification of surface rock and indicates vegetation changes which may be related to underlying mineralisation. It also identifies geological structures, such as faults and folds, with which ore deposits may be associated. Mineral rights are secured at this stage. In most countries a formal system of granting prospecting licences exists. A licence entitles a company to undertake exploration work over a specified time period in a designated, often very large area.

The basis of all effective geological exploration is maps.

Preliminary studies and reconnaissance surveys

Geophysical and geochemical surveys are conducted over an entire region, with widely spaced samples or readings, to test the ground selection. Areas with anomalous results relative to the surroundings may indicate the presence of mineralisation and merit further investigation. A geological anomaly produces measurable variations in properties such as electrical conductivity, magnetism, natural radioactivity or gravitational effects. Geophysical surveys are often carried out from light aircraft or helicopters with minimal environmental impact. In certain wilderness areas flight restrictions may be imposed during breeding and nesting seasons. Aerial photographs are taken and together with geological mapping give important clues to the potential for mineralisation.

Geochemical surveys involve the collection and analysis of water, stream sediment. soil, vegetation and rock outcrop samples for metal content. Elevated metal levels may indicate that rocks containing mineralisation occur in the area. Soil and sediment anomalies have often been transported from the mineralised area by erosion, weathering, glaciation or leaching. Computer modelling produces composite maps of geochemical and geophysical anomalies to define areas for further exploration.

The follow up

Each anomalous area is analysed. Chemical and physical tests are carried out to confirm an anomaly and identify its cause. This may last many months, during which strict measures are taken to avoid disturbing the environment, wildlife and agriculture. The work has minimal impact, with most surveys carried out on foot or by light vehicles. Consultations are held with landowners and the local community to agree access, explain the process and discuss any concerns Special care is taken gaining access ro ecologically sensitive areas.

Some anomalies may be caused by man-made structures and unrelated to mineralisation. Samples and geophysical measurements are taken on a grid system over the area of interest and the results mapped. These give information about the size, depth and attitude of a subsurface deposit and are assessed relative to the geology and topography to select drill sites.

 

Target testing/exploratory drilling

Drilling tests confirm whether the identified anomalies are caused by underlying mineralisation. A typical programme involves drilling from two to a dozen small holes over an area of several square miles. Drilling is usually carried out by contractors and may last a few weeks to over a year, depending on the nature of the test target. If results are not encouraging, further work on an entire area may be abandoned and the surface restored.

Sufficient funds must be available to complete a programme and undertake drill core analysis. Most companies spend a high proportion of their exploration budgets on drilling but costs can run into tens of thousands of dollars for each hole. Selection of the most promising prospects from a number of porential targets demands careful cost estimation and comparison.

Evaluation drilling/pre development

Evaluation drilling and trenching is carried out to determine the nature of the mineralised zone. Deposits are often complex so many samples are needed to establish the grade, size and continuity before further financial resources are justified.

Trenching and drilling can disturb the environment so measures are taken to minimise the impact. Water is needed to cool the drill bit and there is potential for accidental spillages of lubricants or diesel from machinery. These materials are carefully stored and sumps are used as oil traps; as much water as possible is re-cycled If the evaluation is not successful the drill and trench sites are carefully restored.

Once a deposit is identified exploration comes to an end, although additional work may continue at any developed mine to establish further reserves.

A conceptual mining project is prepared so that a preliminary feasibility study to decide if the deposit can be mined economically can be undertaken. This includes the size and nature of the mine and processing plant, with approximate construction costs. Alternative extraction and processing methods, the potential environmental and social impacts with proposed protective and mitigatory measures and closure plans, are all examined.

An Environmental Impact Assesment is carried out as part of the full feasibility study prior to the decision being taken to proceed to development. If consent for a mine is grantedby the planning authorities the prospecting licence is changed to a mining licence.
