AIR POWER: A CENTENNIAL APPRAISAL

By Air Vice-Marshal Tony Mason

(Brasseys(UK) 1994) £30, 320 pages

 

 

Like the first cuckoo of Spring, this must be the earliest sighting of a centennial review of the military use of air power. Tony Mason takes his somewhat quirky starting date as the presentation of a paper by Major Fullerton in 1893. Fullerton's paper concluded that "command of the air would be an essential prerequisite for all land and air warfare" The age of air power had dawned.

 

While the book starts with a brief review of key milestones in the development of air power theory and practice, this is not as one might have anticipated merely a chronological review of the history of military air developments. We are taken very rapidly into the recent past and the post-Cold War world. It is a pleasant surprise to find a chapter devoted to arms control and air forces. This is an area not widely understood and with little literature for reference. The author has done a great service in bringing together the events of the hectic period when aircraft were brought into the CFE process.

 

To examine the breadth of requirements for air power, he marks the Gulf War and Bosnia as either end of a spectrum, and examines the lessons of each in some detail. From the difficulties of Bosnia, the reader is directed on to the current and possible future developments in Russia. Here the analysis becomes more speculative, but is always compelling given the author's depth of knowledge of the Russian air forces. Finally, he turns to what he calls the era of differential air power. He explains why the United States will remain a nation with a quite different air power quality from all other states. Future air operations will therefore be characterised by whether the United States is involved or not.

 

The book is a fascinating read, but the reader will need to be reasonably conversant with the main threads of the history of air power beforehand. The ideas of Douhet and Mitchell are assumed to be familiar territory. Two themes run through this appraisal. First, there is a recurrent tendency for air power doctrine to run ahead of technical capability: this is trap which can still affect the enthusiast. Second, the terrible waste in resources which have stemmed from inter-Service rivalries in many countries is well documented. Looking to the future, Tony Mason reminds us that "among the many resources which contribute to effective air power, only one - people - actually appreciates, rather than depreciates with time." All in all, this is an excellent, thought-provoking book with new insights and research. My only slight criticism would be that the examples may be rather too often from the UK, but I doubt that his home audience will mind.
